## II. SIGNIFICANT FINDINGS BY DIMENSION

### Finding 1: The Accidental Birth of Psychopharmacology for Children (1937)

**Claim:** In 1937, psychiatrist Charles Bradley at the Emma Pendleton Bradley Hospital in Providence, Rhode Island, discovered that Benzedrine (amphetamine sulfate) dramatically improved school performance and behavior in children with behavioral disorders — entirely by accident while attempting to treat post-pneumoenephalogram headaches.

**Source:** *The History of Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder* (NCBI/PMC); *Bradley's Benzedrine Studies on Children with Behavioral Disorders* (Yale J Biol Med, 2011)

**URL:** https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3000907/

**Date:** 1937 (original); 2011 (retrospective analysis)

**Excerpt:** "Bradley attempted to treat the headaches by stimulating the choroid plexus with benzedrine which was 'the most potent stimulant available at the time.' However, benzedrine had a negligible effect on the headaches, but caused a striking improvement in behavior and school performance in some of the children... 'The most spectacular change in behavior brought about by the use of benzedrine was the remarkably improved school performance of approximately half the children.' (Bradley 1937, p. 582)"

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 2: Bradley's Work Was Ignored for 25 Years

**Claim:** Despite the revolutionary nature of his findings, Bradley's 1937 and 1941 studies went "largely ignored in the field of child psychiatry for nearly 25 years." The field was dominated by psychoanalytic and environmental conceptualizations of misbehavior; pharmacological intervention in children was viewed with suspicion.

**Source:** *Bradley's Benzedrine Studies on Children with Behavioral Disorders* (Yale Journal of Biology and Medicine)

**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3064242/

**Date:** 2011

**Excerpt:** "Bradley's studies went largely ignored in the field of child psychiatry for nearly 25 years. However, they proved to be an important precursor to studies of amphetamines like Ritalin and their use in conditions such as attention deficit hyperactivity disorder."

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 3: Ritalin — From Tennis Court to Global Blockbuster (1944–1955)

**Claim:** Methylphenidate was synthesized in 1944 by Ciba chemist Leandro Panizzon in Basel, Switzerland. Panizzon tested it on himself and his wife Marguerite (nickname "Rita"), who noticed improved tennis performance. He named the drug "Ritaline" after her. It was patented in 1954 and FDA-approved in 1955 for depression, narcolepsy, and fatigue — NOT initially for childhood behavioral disorders.

**Source:** ChemicalBook; Le News (Switzerland); PMC

**URL:** https://www.chemicalbook.com/ChemicalProductProperty_EN_CB1874770.htm; https://lenews.ch/2019/08/23/swiss-fact-ritalin-was-invented-in-switzerland/

**Date:** 1944 (synthesis); 1955 (FDA approval)

**Excerpt:** "In 1944, Leandro Panizzon synthesized methylphenidate in Basel while working for CIBA (now Novartis). He tested the drug on himself and his wife Marguerite, nicknamed Rita. Rita played tennis and the drug improved her performance on the court. Panizzon decided to name the drug Ritalin after his wife."

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 5: Adderall — A Diet Pill Reborn (1960 → 1996)

**Claim:** Adderall's active formulation originated as Obetrol, a racemic 75:25 mixture of d-AMP and l-AMP optical isomers developed by Rexar Pharmaceuticals and approved in 1960 as an appetite suppressant for weight control. It was a commercial failure as a diet drug. In 1994, Richwood Pharmaceuticals acquired the formulation, renamed it "Adderall," and attempted to market it for ADHD. The FDA initially rejected it, citing insufficient evidence. After negotiation, it was re-approved in 1996 — remarkably, "without controlled trials of the effects on children with ADHD."

**Source:** *Psychopharmacology: concepts and opinions about the use of stimulant medications* (PMC); Britannica; Recovery Centers of America

**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2681087/; https://www.britannica.com/topic/Adderall

**Date:** 1960 (Obetrol); 1996 (Adderall approval)

**Excerpt:** "Richwood Pharmaceuticals tried to market a formulation of AMP developed by Rexar Pharmaceuticals and approved for appetite control in 1960 (Obetrol, a racemic 75:25 mixture of the d-AMP and l-AMP optical isomers), with a new name (Adderall)... However, after negotiation with the FDA, Richwood Pharmaceutical received re-approval in 1996 to market Adderall for the treatment of ADHD, even though there were no controlled trials of the effects on children with ADHD."

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 8: Strattera's Black Box Warning (2005) — Suicidal Ideation in Children

**Claim:** In September 2005, the FDA required a black box warning on atomoxetine after pooled analysis of 12 short-term placebo-controlled trials (N > 2,200) revealed that 0.37% (5/1357) of children on atomoxetine reported suicidal ideation versus 0/851 on placebo. One attempted suicide occurred. No such risk was found in adults. The warning had significant chilling effects on prescribing, though subsequent analyses in the 2010s found the absolute risk to be extremely small and comparable to methylphenidate.

**Source:** WHO Drug Information; CMAJ; FDA Prescribing Information

**URL:** https://iris.who.int/bitstream/handle/10665/73598/19_4_2005.pdf; https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1316159/

**Date:** September 29, 2005

**Excerpt:** "Pooled analyses of short-term (6 to 18 weeks) placebo-controlled trials of atomoxetine in children and adolescents (a total of 12 trials involving over 2200 patients) have revealed a greater risk of suicidal ideation early during treatment in those receiving atomoxetine compared to placebo. The average risk of suicidal ideation in patients receiving atomoxetine was 0.4% (5/1357 patients), compared to none in placebo-treated patients (851 patients)."

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 9: Concerta/OROS (2000) — The Osmotic Release Revolution

**Claim:** OROS (Osmotic Release Oral System) methylphenidate, marketed as Concerta, was approved in 2000 and represented the longest-acting MPH formulation available. Its ascending pharmacokinetic profile was designed to match natural cortisol rhythms and avoid the peaks-and-troughs of immediate-release formulations. It cannot be crushed or snorted, offering some abuse-deterrence. It was the first long-acting formulation to gain "almost immediate acceptance in clinical practice."

**Source:** *Therapeutics Initiative* (UBC); *Nature Reviews Drug Discovery* (supplementary)

**URL:** https://www.ti.ubc.ca/2010/04/23/oros-methylphenidate-concerta-treatment-children-and-adults-adhd/

**Date:** 2000

**Excerpt:** "OROS formulation of methylphenidate cannot be expected to eliminate the ability of prescription recipients to abuse the drug... There is possibly less drug diversion and abuse when compared to short acting MPH or dextroamphetamine since the outer shell of Concerta is impenetrable to chewing and even if one were to break open the formulation, the inner contents is a paste-like substance so less suitable for intra-nasal administration."

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 10: Vyvanse (2007) — The Prodrug Designed to Resist Abuse

**Claim:** Lisdexamfetamine dimesylate (Vyvanse; New River Pharmaceuticals/Shire) was approved by the FDA in February 2007 as a prodrug of D-amphetamine. It requires enzymatic hydrolysis in the body (primarily by red blood cells) to become active, producing a gradual rise in plasma levels, limiting peaks and troughs, and reducing abuse potential. The molecule is pharmacologically inactive until metabolized, making it unsuitable for intranasal or intravenous abuse.

**Source:** *Nature Reviews Drug Discovery*; *Synapse/PatSnap*

**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/nrd2315; https://synapse.patsnap.com/article/what-is-the-approval-history-and-clinical-development-pathway-of-vyvanse

**Date:** February 2007

**Excerpt:** "Lisdexamfetamine dimesylate (Vyvanse; New River Pharmaceuticals/Shire), a prodrug of D-amphetamine, was approved by the US FDA in February 2007 for the treatment of attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder... The prodrug nature limits peaks and troughs in drug exposure, reducing abuse potential and providing sustained symptom control throughout the day."

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 11: Alpha-2 Agonists — From Off-Label Hypertension Drugs to FDA-Approved ADHD Treatments (2009–2010)

Prior to these approvals, alpha-2 agonists had been used off-label for ADHD for over 25 years, primarily for children with tic disorders or stimulant intolerance. ### Finding 12: Neurofeedback — From Lubar's 1976 Single-Case Experiment to JAMA's 2024 Verdict

**Claim:** The neurofeedback lineage for ADHD began with Joel Lubar and M.N. Shouse's 1976 preliminary report in *Biofeedback and Self-Regulation*, documenting that a hyperkinetic child could learn to increase sensorimotor rhythm (SMR; 12–14 Hz) via EEG biofeedback, with concurrent improvements in motor inhibition. When the training was reversed (rewarding 4–7 Hz theta instead), behavior worsened. Despite nearly 50 years of research, a 2024 JAMA Psychiatry meta-analysis of 38 RCTs (N=2,472) concluded: "RCTs using probably blinded outcomes do not support the use of neurofeedback as a stand-alone ADHD treatment." Only processing speed showed small improvement (SMD 0.35). Methylphenidate was significantly superior (SMD -0.68 to -0.74).

**Source:** *JAMA Psychiatry* (2024); *Biofeedback and Self-Regulation* (1976); *PMC* neurofeedback reviews

**URL:** https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamapsychiatry/fullarticle/2827733; https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/990355/

**Date:** 1976 (origin); December 11, 2024 (JAMA meta-analysis)

**Excerpt (1976):** "A substantial increase in SMR motor inhibition, as gauged by laboratory measures of muscular tone (chin EMG) and by a global behavioral assessment in the classroom. Opposite trends in motor inhibition occurred when the training procedure was reversed and feedback presentations were contingent on the production of 4–7 Hz in the absence of 12–14-Hz activity."

**Excerpt (2024 JAMA):** "Probably blinded reports of ADHD total symptoms showed no significant improvement with neurofeedback (k=20; n=1214; SMD, 0.04; 95% CI, −0.10 to 0.18)... methylphenidate had a significantly greater effect than neurofeedback on total ADHD (k=7; n=417; SMD, −0.68; 95% CI, −0.92 to −0.44)."

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 13: Cogmed Working Memory Training — The Rise and Fall of "Brain Training" for ADHD

**Claim:** Cogmed Working Memory Training, developed by Torkel Klingberg and commercialized by Pearson, was initially heralded as a breakthrough non-pharmacological intervention. However, multiple meta-analyses have demonstrated that while children improve on the trained tasks (near transfer), there is no convincing evidence of generalization to untrained cognitive abilities, academic achievement, or real-world ADHD symptoms (far transfer). The 2015 Cortese et al. meta-analysis (EAGG) in *JAACAP* found that moderate improvements in "most proximal" (least blinded) measures dropped to marginal or non-significant levels when "probably blinded" outcomes were analyzed. A 2023 update found effect sizes had actually *decreased* over time as trial quality improved. The consensus: "no evidence to support the use of CCT in its current forms as a stand-alone treatment for core ADHD symptoms."

**Source:** *Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry* (Cortese 2015); *PMC* (CCT meta-analysis 2023)

**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10208955/; https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7065563/

**Date:** 2015; 2023

**Excerpt:** "This comprehensive and well-powered meta-analysis found no evidence to support the use of CCT in its current forms as a stand-alone treatment for core ADHD symptoms. In our pre-registered analyses, effects on PBLIND measures of core ADHD features were limited to a significant, but small, short-term effect on inattention symptoms... The small size of the effect suggested limited clinical significance when compared to the size of short-term effects of methylphenidate on ADHD core symptoms."

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 14: EndeavorRx (2020) — The First FDA-Cleared Video Game Prescription

**Claim:** In June 2020, Akili Interactive's EndeavorRx (formerly Project: EVO) received FDA De Novo clearance (DEN200026) as the first digital therapeutic for any pediatric condition, the first video game approved by the FDA as a treatment for any medical condition, and (later) the first over-the-counter digital treatment. It grew out of Adam Gazzaley's UCSF lab's Neuroracer, which was published as a *Nature* cover story in 2013 showing cognitive improvements in older adults. The pivotal STARS-ADHD trial (N=348) was published in *Lancet Digital Health* (2020). Despite the regulatory milestone, commercial adoption has been limited — Akili reported only $323,000 in revenue for 2022, with 1,801 prescriptions in Q4 2022. In 2024, Akili was acquired by Virtual Therapeutics for $34 million, far below its $1 billion SPAC valuation.

**Source:** FDA; *Lancet Digital Health*; *MobiHealthNews*; *Regulatory Affairs Professionals Society*

**URL:** https://www.raps.org/resource/first-video-game-based-treatment-gets-go-ahead-fro.html; https://www.mobihealthnews.com/news/akili-reports-first-public-full-year-earnings-it-focuses-adhd

**Date:** June 15, 2020

**Excerpt:** "FDA says the device is the 'first digital therapeutic intended to improve symptoms associated with ADHD, as well as the first game-based therapeutic granted marketing authorization by the FDA for any type of condition.'... **Confidence:** High

### Finding 15: The Feingold Diet (1970s) — The First Major Nutritional Paradigm

**Claim:** In the 1970s, Dr. Benjamin Feingold, a San Francisco pediatrician and allergist, proposed that artificial food colors, flavors, preservatives (BHA, BHT, TBHQ), and naturally occurring salicylates could cause or exacerbate hyperactivity. His elimination diet (the "Kaiser Permanente diet") gained massive public attention but produced mixed research results. A 1982 NIH consensus conference recommended further study. The approach is still promoted today by the Feingold Association. European research has been more supportive than U.S. studies, with the Southampton studies (2004, 2007) contributing to EU food labeling requirements for six dyes with warning labels.

**Source:** *PMC* — Restriction and Elimination Diets in ADHD Treatment; WebMD

**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4322780/; https://www.webmd.com/add-adhd/childhood-adhd/what-is-the-feingold-diet

**Date:** 1970s (origin); 1982 (NIH consensus)

**Excerpt:** "Feingold was an allergist, so his predisposition was to evaluate for potential allergens in patients. He suggested initially that children who are allergic to aspirin (which contains salicylates) may be reactive to synthetic food colors as well as naturally occurring salicylates... In 1982, the National Institutes of Health convened a consensus development conference on defined diets and childhood hyperactivity, which recommended further study."

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 16: Omega-3 Fatty Acids — Modest but Documented Effects

**Claim:** Multiple meta-analyses confirm that children with ADHD have lower blood levels of omega-3 fatty acids (particularly EPA and DHA) than controls (g=0.42, p<.001). Supplementation produces small but statistically significant improvements in ADHD composite symptoms (g=0.26, p<.001), with effects most reliable on hyperactivity. Higher EPA doses (≥500 mg/day) correlate with greater efficacy. The 2011 Bloch & Qawasmi meta-analysis in *JAACAP* (10 trials, N=699) concluded: "Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation, particularly with higher doses of eicosapentaenoic acid, was modestly effective in the treatment of ADHD." However, relative efficacy is "modest compared with currently available pharmacotherapies."

**Source:** *Clinical Psychology Review* (2014); *JAACAP* (2011); *Nature* Translational Psychiatry (2019)

**URL:** https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4321799/; https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21961774/

**Date:** 2011; 2014; 2019

**Excerpt:** "Omega-3 levels are reduced in children with ADHD. Dietary supplementation appears to create modest improvements in symptoms. There is sufficient evidence to consider Omega-3 fatty acids as a possible supplement to established therapies... higher doses of EPA within essential fatty acids supplements was significantly associated with increased efficacy in treating ADHD symptoms."

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 17: Exercise — From Folk Wisdom to Meta-Analytic Support

**Claim:** Physical exercise as an ADHD intervention has moved from anecdote to evidence-supported adjunctive treatment. A 2023 umbrella review of 106 study estimates found "highly suggestive evidence" that exercise improves cognitive flexibility, inattention, and inhibitory control in children and adolescents with ADHD. A 2022 network meta-analysis (59 studies, N=1,757) found all exercise types effective for executive functions (SMD=1.15), with open-skill activities (e.g., martial arts, team sports) producing the greatest benefits. The mechanism is hypothesized to involve exercise-induced increases in dopamine, norepinephrine, serotonin, and BDNF — similar to stimulant medications but via natural physiological pathways.

**Source:** *EClinicalMedicine* (2023 umbrella review); *Psychological Medicine* (2022 network meta-analysis); *Pediatric Research* (2025)

**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10432969/; https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37033046/

**Date:** 2022–2025

**Excerpt:** "There was highly suggestive evidence that cognitive flexibility, inattention problem, and inhibitory control were improved by physical exercise... All types of physical exercise were effective in improving EFs (SMD = 1.15, 95% CI: 0.83 to 1.46), and open-skill activities which require participants to react in a dynamically changing and externally paced environment induced the most incredible benefits for executive functions."

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 18: Mindfulness/MBCT — From Fringe to Modest Evidence Base

**Claim:** Mindfulness-based interventions (MBIs) for ADHD have accumulated enough evidence to support their use as complementary interventions. A 2025 meta-analysis of 10 controlled trials in adults found significant improvements in self-reported ADHD symptoms (SMD=0.48), observer-rated symptoms (SMD=0.32), and functional outcomes (SMD=0.56). For children, a 2022 meta-analysis of 12 RCTs found a large effect on ADHD symptoms (g=0.77), with larger effects for older children/adolescents. However, effects on mindfulness skills themselves, negative affect, and positive affect were inconclusive or non-significant — suggesting the mechanism of benefit may not be through enhanced "mindfulness" per se.

**Source:** *PubMed* (2025 adult meta-analysis); *PMC* (2022 children meta-analysis)

**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40958241/; https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9690476/

**Date:** 2022; 2025

**Excerpt:** "Statistically significant improvements were observed in self-reported ADHD symptoms (SMD = 0.48, 95% CI [0.19, 0.76]), observer-rated ADHD symptoms (SMD = 0.32, 95% CI [0.09, 0.56]), and functional outcomes (SMD = 0.56, 95% CI [0.22, 0.90]). However, there were no significant effects on mindfulness skills... These findings support the utility of MBIs as complementary interventions for ADHD."

**Confidence:** High

### Finding 19: TMS for ADHD — Emerging but Preliminary Evidence

**Claim:** A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) for ADHD found that TMS significantly improved inattention, hyperactivity/impulsivity, and total symptom scores after 3–6 weeks of intervention, with improvements in inattention persisting at 1-month follow-up. The left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) was the primary stimulation target. Adverse events were minor. However, the authors caution that heterogeneity in protocols and small sample sizes limit definitive conclusions.

**Source:** *Brain and Behavior* (2024 systematic review)

**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11743978/

**Date:** 2024

**Excerpt:** "TMS significantly improved the inattention, hyperactivity/impulsivity, and total symptom scores in ADHD patients with minor adverse events... TMS could facilitate neural plasticity. A review of studies on the role of TMS in neurological disorders also suggested that the therapeutic effects of TMS could last from a few days to a few weeks."

**Confidence:** Medium

### Finding 20: tDCS for ADHD — Promising Laboratory Effects, Uncertain Clinical Translation

**Claim:** Transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) studies in ADHD have shown that anodal stimulation over the left DLPFC combined with cathodal stimulation over the right ventromedial prefrontal cortex improves emotional pre-potent inhibition, emotional working memory, and cold executive functions in children with ADHD. A 2024 study found both real stimulation protocols improved No-Go accuracy and 1-back task performance versus sham. However, at least one 2024 RCT (Guimarães et al.) found no positive impact, highlighting the field's inconsistency. The 2020 systematic review by Salehinejad et al. called for standardized protocols.

**Source:** *Scientific Reports* (2024); *Neuroscience Bulletin* (2020 systematic review)

**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-64886-9; https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33025602/

**Date:** 2020; 2024

**Excerpt:** "Emotion regulation improvement was found during both stimulation protocols. In sum, we found anodal left dlPFC/cathodal right vmPFC and the reversed electrode placement stimulation improves emotion regulation in children with ADHD."

**Confidence:** Medium

### Finding 21: Psychedelic Microdosing for ADHD — The 2025 LSD Milestone

**Claim:** In March 2025, *JAMA Psychiatry* published the first double-blind, placebo-controlled Phase 2A RCT of repeated low-dose LSD for adult ADHD (N=53). Conducted at University Hospital Basel and Maastricht University, the trial used 5–20 μg LSD every 3 days for 6 weeks. Prior naturalistic studies (Haijen et al., 2022, N=233 prospective) had found adults self-medicating ADHD with psychedelic microdosing reported symptom decreases and well-being increases. The JAMA trial represents the first rigorous test of this emerging paradigm, though results were not yet fully published in the search excerpts.

**Source:** *JAMA Psychiatry* (2025); *Neuroscience Applied* (2022)

## III. PARADIGM PHASES: How ADHD Treatment Philosophy Evolved

### Phase 1: Accidental Discovery (1937–1954)
- **Philosophy:** Psychodynamic/environmental dominant; pharmacology accidental
- **Key event:** Bradley's Benzedrine discovery
- **Paradigm:** "Why do stimulants calm hyperactive children?" — Bradley's paradox

### Phase 2: Pharmaceutical Development (1955–1970)
- **Philosophy:** Medicalization begins; amphetamines and methylphenidate enter psychiatry
- **Key events:** Ritalin approval (1955), Dexedrine (1949), Adderall origin as Obetrol (1960)
- **Paradigm:** Stimulants as "chemical straitjackets" — first backlash emerges

### Phase 3: Controversy and Backlash (1971–1990)
- **Philosophy:** Skepticism about over-medicalization; parent activism (Feingold diet)
- **Key events:** Schedule II classification (1971), *The Myth of the Hyperactive Child* (1975), neurofeedback emergence (Lubar 1976)
- **Paradigm:** "Is ADHD real?" — anti-psychiatry movement challenges stimulant use

### Phase 4: The Stimulant Boom (1991–2001)
- **Philosophy:** Neurobiological model dominant; ADHD as "brain disorder"
- **Key events:** DSM-IV (1994) expands criteria, Adderall revival (1996), Concerta (2000), Adderall XR (2001)
- **Paradigm:** Medication as first-line treatment; prescription rates explode

### Phase 5: Diversification and Non-Stimulant Era (2002–2010)
- **Philosophy:** Need for alternatives to Schedule II stimulants
- **Key events:** Strattera (2002), Intuniv (2009), Kapvay (2010)
- **Paradigm:** "Not all patients can or should take stimulants"

### Phase 6: Safety Reckoning and Long-Acting Revolution (2005–2015)
- **Philosophy:** Risk-benefit reassessment; abuse deterrence becomes priority
- **Key events:** Black box warnings (2005), Vyvanse prodrug (2007), Mydayis triple-bead (2017)
- **Paradigm:** Formulation innovation as competitive strategy; once-daily dosing standard

### Phase 7: Digital and Non-Pharmacological Renaissance (2013–2025)
- **Philosophy:** Beyond pills — neuroplasticity-based interventions
- **Key events:** Neuroracer *Nature* cover (2013), EndeavorRx FDA clearance (2020), LSD microdosing RCT (2025)
- **Paradigm:** "Can we train the brain rather than drug it?" — mixed evidence so far

## V. RESEARCH ARCHAEOLOGY NOTES

### What Was Suppressed or Under-Emphasized:
1. **Bradley's 1937 findings were ignored for 25 years** — the field of child psychiatry was dominated by psychoanalytic and environmental theories; biological treatments were viewed with suspicion.

2. **Adderall was approved in 1996 without controlled pediatric trials** — an unusual regulatory pathway based on "negotiation" and physician testimony rather than RCT evidence.

3. **Cogmed and working memory training were heavily marketed** with claims of broad cognitive benefit before the 2015 EAGG meta-analysis demonstrated no far-transfer effects on real-world ADHD symptoms.

4. **Neurofeedback practitioners claimed "Level 1 — Best Support" status** (PracticeWise 2017–2018) based on non-blinded studies; the 2024 JAMA Psychiatry meta-analysis using blinded outcomes refuted this.

5. **Pharmaceutical company funding dominates ADHD drug research** — a 2011 Danish study noted that manufacturers fund almost all studies, and researchers frequently receive company contributions, raising questions about bias in safety assessments.
