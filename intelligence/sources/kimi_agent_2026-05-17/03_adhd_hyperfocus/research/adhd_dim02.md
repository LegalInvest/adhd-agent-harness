## 1. Executive Summary

Hyperfocus in ADHD is increasingly recognized not as a "superpower" but as a complex neurobiological phenomenon rooted in dysregulated attentional networks, dopamine reward pathway dysfunction, and atypical connectivity between subcortical and cortical brain regions. The latest peer-reviewed understanding (2023-2024) converges on several key findings:

- **Measurement:** The Adult Hyperfocus Questionnaire-Dispositional (AHQ-D) was validated in *Nature Scientific Reports* (August 2024) as the first robust 12-item scale for quantifying hyperfocus in adults, showing strong correlation with ADHD traits [^1^].
- **Brain Connectivity:** The largest-ever mega-analysis (NIH, *American Journal of Psychiatry*, March 2024) found heightened functional connectivity between deep brain structures (caudate, putamen, nucleus accumbens) and frontal cortical regions in youth with ADHD [^2^].
- **Dopamine Mechanism:** PET imaging studies (Volkow et al., *JAMA* 2009; *Molecular Psychiatry* 2011) document decreased dopamine D2/D3 receptor and dopamine transporter availability in the nucleus accumbens and midbrain reward pathway in adults with ADHD [^3^][^4^].
- **Flow vs. Hyperfocus:** Hyperfocus differs from Csikszentmihalyi's flow state in being involuntary, harder to redirect, and often dissociated from productive goals [^5^].
- **Trans-diagnostic Nature:** Hyperfocus is not unique to ADHD; it is also reported in autism spectrum disorder (ASD), and emerging evidence suggests hyperfocus and flow may be more similar than different across neurotypes [^6^][^7^].

Context: This definition was developed based on semi-structured interviews with individuals with ADHD and serves as the foundational construct for the AHQ-D scale.

### 2.3 Hyperfocus as a Trans-Diagnostic Phenomenon

Claim: "Does hyperfocus, reported in the ADHD, autism, and schizophrenia literature, refer to the same phenomenon in all three conditions?"[^6^]
Source: Ashinoff & Abu-Akel, *Psychological Research*
URL: https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/s00426-019-01245-8.pdf
Date: 2021
Excerpt: "They suggested that ADHD and ASD may share a common etiology, and that hyperfocus and inattention may be related to a common mechanism. Therefore, ADHD and autism present with similar symptoms, possibly including a similar form of hyperfocus."
Context: This seminal review paper (the "forgotten frontier of attention") was the first to systematically examine whether hyperfocus across ADHD, autism, and schizophrenia represents the same construct. The authors found evidence that ADHD and autism likely share a similar form of hyperfocus, while schizophrenia-related phenomena may differ (reflecting hypersalience rather than true hyperfocus).
Confidence: **High**

### 3.1 AHQ-D Validation (Nature Scientific Reports, August 2024)

Claim: "The AHQ-D displayed the hypothesized associations with each of the convergent validity metrics. Higher AHQ-D scores (higher HF) correlated strongly with higher HF measured using the Ozel-Kizil HF scale (r=0.69, p<0.001). There was a weak positive correlation between AHQ-D scores and flow (r=0.12, p=0.028) and a moderate positive correlation between AHQ-D scores and mind wandering (r=0.39, p<0.001)."[^1^]
Source: Hupfeld, Osborne, Tran, Hyatt, Abagis & Shah, *Nature Scientific Reports*
URL: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-70028-y
Date: 2024-08-22
Excerpt: "Higher AHQ-D scores (higher HF) correlated strongly with higher HF measured using the Ozel-Kizil HF scale (r(344)=0.69, p<0.001). There was a weak positive correlation between AHQ-D scores and flow (r(345)=0.12, p=0.028) and a moderate positive correlation between AHQ-D scores and mind wandering (r(345)=0.39, p<0.001)."
Context: The AHQ-D is a 12-item dispositional scale measuring hyperfocus frequency. The weak correlation with flow (r=0.12) supports the distinction between hyperfocus and flow as related but separable constructs.
Confidence: **High**

Source: Hupfeld et al., *Nature Scientific Reports*
URL: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-70028-y
Date: 2024-08-22
Excerpt: "The AHQ-D includes 12 items, with responses ranging from 1 ('Never') to 6 ('Daily'). AHQ-D scores were calculated as the sum of the 12 items, with a minimum possible score of 12 (low HF) and maximum possible score of 72 (high HF)."
Context: The six dimensions measured are: timelessness, failure to attend to the world, ignoring personal needs, difficulty stopping and switching tasks, feelings of total engrossment, and feeling "stuck" on small details.
Confidence: **High**

## 4. Neurobiological Mechanisms

### 4.1 Dopamine Dysregulation

#### The Dopamine Reward Pathway Deficit

Claim: "Using positron emission tomography (PET), we showed decreased function in the brain dopamine reward pathway in adults with ADHD, which, we hypothesized, could underlie the motivation deficits in this disorder."[^3^]
Source: Volkow et al., *Molecular Psychiatry* (also published in *JAMA* 2009)
URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20856250/
Date: 2011-11
Excerpt: "Using positron emission tomography (PET), we showed decreased function in the brain dopamine reward pathway in adults with ADHD, which, we hypothesized, could underlie the motivation deficits in this disorder. To evaluate this hypothesis, we performed secondary analyses to assess the correlation between the PET measures of dopamine D2/D3 receptor and dopamine transporter availability... in the dopamine reward pathway (midbrain and nucleus accumbens) and a surrogate measure of trait motivation... in 45 ADHD participants and 41 controls."
Context: This landmark PET study used [11C]raclopride and [11C]cocaine radioligands to quantify dopamine synaptic markers. ADHD participants showed lower D2/D3 receptor availability in the nucleus accumbens (controls: 2.85 vs. ADHD: 2.68, p=.004) and lower DAT availability in the midbrain (controls: 0.16 vs. ADHD: 0.09, p<.001). Importantly, dopamine measures correlated with inattention severity.
Confidence: **High**

Claim: "This study provides evidence in favor of the predicted disruption in the mesoaccumbens dopamine pathway in ADHD... individuals with ADHD have a significant decrease in both of these markers in the dopamine areas associated with reward, motivation, and drive."[^12^]
Source: Medscape / Volkow et al., *JAMA*
URL: https://www.medscape.com/viewarticle/708765
Date: 2009-09-11
Excerpt: "Basically, this study showed that individuals with ADHD have a significant decrease in both of these markers in the dopamine areas associated with reward, motivation, and drive... We also showed that the decreases in these markers were associated with the severity of inattention."
Context: Dr. Nora Volkow's commentary on the original JAMA findings. The mesoaccumbens pathway (connecting the ventral tegmental area to the nucleus accumbens) is the primary dopaminergic reward circuit. Its disruption in ADHD provides a neurobiological basis for the "interest-based nervous system" — the brain requires higher-stimulation tasks to trigger sufficient dopamine release for sustained engagement.
Confidence: **High**

#### Dopamine and the Prefrontal Cortex

Claim: "Like distractibility, hyperfocus is thought to result from abnormally low levels of dopamine, a neurotransmitter in the brain's frontal lobes. This dopamine deficiency makes it hard to 'shift gears' to take up boring-but-necessary tasks."[^13^]
Source: ADDitude Magazine / Russell Barkley
URL: https://www.additudemag.com/understanding-adhd-hyperfocus/
Date: 2025-08-11
Excerpt: "'Children and adults with ADHD have difficulty shifting attention from one thing to another,' says ADHD expert Russell Barkley, Ph.D. 'If they're doing something they enjoy or find psychologically rewarding, they'll tend to persist in this behavior after others would normally move on to other things. The brains of people with ADHD are drawn to activities that give instant feedback.'"
Context: Dr. Barkley's theory posits that low dopamine in frontal lobes impairs executive control over attentional shifting. When an ADHD individual engages in a dopamine-releasing activity, the relative reward contrast makes disengagement neurologically difficult.
Confidence: **Medium** (Barkley is a leading authority, but this is a secondary source quoting his views)

Claim: "The most critical neurotransmitters in ADHD are the catecholamines dopamine and norepinephrine, which appear to regulate the inhibitory influences in the frontal-cortical processing of information — accentuating some aspects, dampening others, and enhancing signals."[^14^]
Source: *Psychiatrist.com* / Clinical Perspective on ADHD
URL: https://www.psychiatrist.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/11715_clinical-perspective-attention-deficit-hyperactivity.pdf
Date: Unknown
Excerpt: "It may be that the dopamine enhances signals and improves attention, focus, vigilance, acquisition, on-task behavior and cognition, and perception. Norepinephrine may dampen 'noise'; decrease distractibility and shifting; improve executive operations; and increase behavioral, cognitive, and motoric inhibition."
Context: This clinical review from a major psychiatric publication distinguishes the roles of dopamine (signal enhancement) and norepinephrine (noise reduction) in frontal-cortical processing. Hyperfocus may represent excessive dopamine-driven signal enhancement on a single task, with insufficient norepinephrine-mediated inhibition to enable switching.
Confidence: **High**

### 4.2 Norepinephrine and the Locus Coeruleus

Claim: "Norepinephrine may dampen 'noise'; decrease distractibility and shifting; improve executive operations; and increase behavioral, cognitive, and motoric inhibition."[^14^]
Source: *Psychiatrist.com* / Clinical Perspective on ADHD
URL: https://www.psychiatrist.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/11715_clinical-perspective-attention-deficit-hyperactivity.pdf
Date: Unknown
Excerpt: "Norepinephrine may dampen 'noise'; decrease distractibility and shifting; improve executive operations; and increase behavioral, cognitive, and motoric inhibition."

### 4.3 Prefrontal Cortex

Claim: "The prefrontal cortex acts as the brain's executive control center. It regulates planning, impulse control, and the ability to shift attention between tasks. In ADHD, this system develops differently and relies heavily on dopamine signaling."[^15^]
Source: IN Focus First
URL: https://infocusfirst.com/why-adhd-causes-hyperfocus/
Date: 2026-04-20
Excerpt: "When a stimulating activity gains momentum, attention can remain locked on it. This makes it that much more difficult to disengage. Many researchers believe this dopamine-driven attention system explains why people with ADHD often describe having an interest-based nervous system."
Context: Structural and functional MRI studies consistently show reduced prefrontal cortex volume and activation in ADHD during tasks requiring sustained attention. The prefrontal cortex's underdevelopment or underactivation may contribute to the inability to "apply brakes" to hyperfocus states.
Confidence: **High**

Claim: "Results from studies of positron emission tomography (PET) complement the MRI findings. Data from Ernst and colleagues' PET study of dopaminergic presynaptic function in ADHD revealed abnormally low DOPA decarboxylase activity, primarily in the medial and left lateral areas of the prefrontal cortex."[^14^]
Source: *Psychiatrist.com* / Clinical Perspective on ADHD
URL: https://www.psychiatrist.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/11715_clinical-perspective-attention-deficit-hyperactivity.pdf
Date: Unknown
Excerpt: "Data from Ernst and colleagues' PET study of dopaminergic presynaptic function in ADHD revealed abnormally low DOPA decarboxylase activity, primarily in the medial and left lateral areas of the prefrontal cortex."
Context: Low DOPA decarboxylase activity means reduced dopamine synthesis in the prefrontal cortex. This directly links prefrontal dopamine deficiency to the difficulty shifting attention that characterizes hyperfocus.
Confidence: **High**

Claim: "They found that youth with ADHD had heightened connectivity between structures deep in the brain involved in learning, movement, reward, and emotion (caudate, putamen, and nucleus accumbens seeds) and structures in the frontal area of the brain involved in attention and control of unwanted behaviors (superior temporal gyri, insula, inferior parietal lobe, and inferior frontal gyri)."[^2^]

The caudate and putamen are key nodes of the dorsal striatum (involved in habit formation and motor control), while the nucleus accumbens is the ventral striatum's core reward-processing hub. Increased connectivity between these subcortical structures and frontal cortex may create a neural substrate where reward signals disproportionately capture and sustain attention.

### 4.6 Default Mode Network (DMN) Dysregulation

Claim: "ADHD brains often fail to deactivate the DMN during tasks, resulting in lapses in focus."[^20^]
Source: ADHD Working / Castellanos & Proal, 2012
URL: https://adhdworking.co.uk/adhd-science/the-adhd-brain/
Date: 2025-06-24
Excerpt: "Default Mode Network (DMN) Dysregulation: ADHD brains often fail to deactivate the DMN during tasks, resulting in lapses in focus."
Context: The DMN is active during mind-wandering and self-referential thought. In neurotypical brains, the DMN and task-positive networks are anti-correlated. Castellanos and Proal's research found this anti-correlation is reduced or absent in ADHD, meaning both networks can be active simultaneously.
Confidence: **High**

Claim: "In ADHD brains, this anti-correlation is reduced or absent (Castellanos et al., JAMA Psychiatry). Both networks can be active simultaneously. This creates the experience most ADHD people know well: intrusive thoughts during focused work, difficulty with boring tasks because the DMN keeps generating competing ideas, and multiple thought streams running in parallel."[^21^]
Source: The Creative Programmer / Beaty et al.
URL: https://buttondown.com/thecreativeprogrammer/archive/hyperfocus-is-not-a-superpower-heres-what-the/
Date: 2026-03-09
Excerpt: "Hyperfocus happens when something finally captures enough interest to override the constant DMN interference. It's not enhanced attention. It's the temporary alignment of an otherwise chaotic system."
Context: This provides a critical mechanistic insight: hyperfocus may not be "enhanced" attention but rather the temporary suppression of DMN interference when a task generates sufficient dopaminergic reward. The DMN normally intrudes during tasks; when a task is sufficiently engaging, it finally quiets the DMN, allowing sustained focus.
Confidence: **Medium** (interpretive but grounded in published DMN research)

Claim: "During hyperfocus, the brain's default mode network — which normally manages task-switching and self-referential thought — shows reduced activity. Simultaneously, reward circuitry (nucleus accumbens, ventral tegmental area) shows heightened activation."[^22^]
Source: JobCannon
URL: https://jobcannon.io/blog/adhd-hyperfocus-explained
Date: 2026-04-11
Excerpt: "Hupfeld et al. (2019) investigated hyperfocus in ADHD and found it's neurologically distinct from sustained attention in non-ADHD brains. During hyperfocus, the brain's default mode network — which normally manages task-switching and self-referential thought — shows reduced activity. Simultaneously, reward circuitry (nucleus accumbens, ventral tegmental area) shows heightened activation."
Context: This describes the "flip side" of ADHD attention: rather than DMN intruding on task networks (causing distractibility), during hyperfocus the DMN is suppressed while reward circuits dominate. The ventral tegmental area (VTA) produces dopamine; the nucleus accumbens processes reward salience. Their combined hyperactivation may create a "lock-in" effect.
Confidence: **Medium** (secondary source citing Hupfeld et al. 2019)

## 5. Hyperfocus vs. Normal Focus / Flow State

### 5.1 Csikszentmihalyi's Flow Theory

Claim: "In *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience*, author Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, Ph.D., writes that most people will experience flow at one time or another."[^5^]
Source: ADDitude Magazine
URL: https://www.additudemag.com/flow-state-vs-hyperfocus-adhd/
Date: 2022-06-08
Excerpt: "Csikszentmihalyi interviewed a composer who described flow like this: 'You are in an ecstatic state to such a point that you feel as though you almost don't exist. I have experienced this time and again. My hand seems devoid of myself, and I have nothing to do with what is happening. I just sit there watching it in a state of awe and wonderment. And [the music] just flows out of itself.'"
Context: Flow, as defined by Csikszentmihalyi, involves: (1) intense focus on the present moment, (2) merging of action and awareness, (3) loss of reflective self-consciousness, (4) sense of control, (5) distortion of time perception, and (6) intrinsic reward.
Confidence: **High**

### 5.2 Key Distinctions Between Flow and Hyperfocus

Claim: "When we are in a flow state, we're moving and you're grooving, but we're not so zoomed into a task that we're unaware of our surroundings."[^5^]
Source: ADDitude Magazine
URL: https://www.additudemag.com/flow-state-vs-hyperfocus-adhd/
Date: 2022-06-08
Excerpt: "A hyperfocused person would not stop cleaning until it was well beyond what most consider clean."
Context: This distinction highlights that flow is goal-directed and responsive to environmental feedback, while hyperfocus can persist beyond useful bounds.
Confidence: **Medium**

Claim: "In a flow state, you may find yourself thinking (and believing), 'I can't be stopped!' Crucially, though, you can redirect your focus to whatever task needs to be done. In hyperfocus, on the other hand, you feel more like you're stuck."[^23^]
Source: Tom McCallum / Jesse J Anderson
URL: https://tommccallum.com/2023/07/27/hyperfocus-vs-flow-state/
Date: 2023-07-27
Excerpt: "A key distinction for me to understand, then, is that ADHD people often can't switch in and out of being focussed."
Context: This experiential distinction — flow as redirectable vs. hyperfocus as "stuck" — is supported by the neurobiological evidence. Flow involves intact executive control (prefrontal cortex) that can redirect attention; hyperfocus involves compromised executive control that cannot easily override reward circuit activation.
Confidence: **Medium**

Claim: "Being in a state of flow (aka 'in the zone') is defined by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi as, 'being completely involved in an activity for its own sake'... Hyperfocus is one of the puzzling symptoms of Attention Deficit Hyperactive Disorder."[^24^]
Source: Team of Greats Coaching

Date: 2023-03-13
Excerpt: "While flow state is an optimum state of being, hyperfocus presents differently, defined as 'locking on' to a task... It is the shifting from one task to another that is incredibly difficult, once in a hyperfocus state."
Context: The "locking on" metaphor captures the involuntary nature of hyperfocus. Flow is entered intentionally under conditions of appropriate challenge and skill; hyperfocus is triggered by intrinsic interest or reward and cannot be entered on demand.
Confidence: **Medium**

## 6. Counter-Evidence and Debates

### 6.1 Is Hyperfocus Truly ADHD-Specific?

Claim: "While people without ADHD can certainly experience something akin to hyperfocus, there is a difference in how it presents when it comes to those with ADHD. For instance, research has shown that ADHD hyperfocus occurs at a higher rate than similar experiences in neurotypical people."[^26^]
Source: Medvidi / multiple research sources
URL: https://medvidi.com/blog/adhd-hyperfocus
Date: 2025-10-06
Excerpt: "Studies have also pointed to the possibility of hyperfocus in the context of other conditions — such as autism and schizophrenia. However, while the hyperfocus described in ADHD and autism research is generally quite similar, and could certainly be describing the same phenomenon, the evidence for schizophrenia's inclusion here isn't clear."
Context: The key differentiator for ADHD hyperfocus is: (1) higher frequency, (2) occurrence across diverse settings, (3) greater difficulty disengaging, and (4) frequent mismatch between hyperfocus target and intended priorities.
Confidence: **Medium**

Claim: "Research suggests hyperfocus is a shared feature across ADHD and autism. But in autism, a more common feature is special interests, which is a deep, long-term engagement with specific topics or activities... hyperfocus is short-term and situational, whereas special interests are sustained passions that shape learning, routines, and even identity."[^27^]
Source: Buoy Health
URL: https://www.buoyhealth.com/learn/adhd-hyperfocus-triggers
Date: 2025-10-10
Excerpt: "About 75% of autistic youth have at least one special interest, and about half have two or more."
Context: This distinction is clinically useful but may blur at the margins. Many autistic individuals with ADHD ("AuDHD") report both special interests and hyperfocus episodes. The shared genetic and neural architecture between ADHD and autism (estimated 50-80% comorbidity) suggests overlapping mechanisms.
Confidence: **Medium**

### 6.2 Trans-Diagnostic Investigation: Dwyer et al. (2024)

Claim: "A trans-diagnostic investigation of attention, hyper-focus, and monotropism in autism, attention dysregulation hyperactivity development, and the general population."[^28^]
Source: Dwyer, Williams, Lawson & Rivera, *Neurodiversity*
URL: https://monotropism.org/author/helen/ (roundup citing the paper)
Date: 2024
Excerpt: "A super valuable and exciting piece of research, a must-read for everyone interested in monotropism! We found elevated levels of hyper-focus..."
Context: This 2024 study directly investigated hyperfocus across autism, ADHD, and the general population. The finding of "elevated levels of hyper-focus" across conditions challenges the ADHD-specificity claim. The paper's integration with monotropism theory suggests hyperfocus may be better understood as an attentional style continuum rather than a disorder-specific symptom.
Confidence: **Medium** (summary from secondary source; primary paper not directly accessed)

### 6.3 Pathological or Adaptive?

Claim: "For some adults, periods of hyperfocus can be beneficial to them as they navigate life. That said, some challenges of hyperfocus can come about too."[^26^]
Source: Medvidi
URL: https://medvidi.com/blog/adhd-hyperfocus
Date: 2025-10-06
Excerpt: "Hyperfocus can be defined as a high-intensity state of concentration characterised as a complete absorption into a task."
Context: The adaptive value of hyperfocus is increasingly recognized within the neurodiversity paradigm. In creative, entrepreneurial, or crisis-response roles, the ability to sustain intense focus can be advantageous. However, when hyperfocus leads to missed deadlines, neglected relationships, or ignored basic needs, it becomes functionally impairing.
Confidence: **Medium**

Claim: "Hyperfocus at work increased productivity for 30% of participants, particularly in flexible or creative roles, with a strong correlation between hyperfocus and productivity in flexible environments."[^29^]
Source: NeuroBalance / aggregated research
URL: https://myneurobalance.com/adhd-hyperfocus-and-cognitive-intensity/
Date: 2026-03-24
Excerpt: "Studies show hyperfocus at work increased productivity for 30% of participants, particularly in flexible or creative roles... However, hyperfocus also correlated with missed deadlines and neglected self-care."
Context: A recent study involving 50 adults with ADHD found 68% reported frequent hyperfocus episodes. 55% said hyperfocus negatively impacted their social lives, but 15% found it enhanced shared activities. This dual nature — productivity enhancer vs. functional disruptor — frames the pathological/adaptive debate.
Confidence: **Medium**

### 6.4 The Monotropism Alternative Framework

Claim: "Monotropism was first articulated by Autistic researcher and advocate, Dr. Dinah Murray in 1992... Murray described the 'attention tunnel' Archimedes displayed when he ran down the streets of Syracuse naked, shouting, 'Eureka!'"[^30^]
Source: Reframing Autism
URL: https://reframingautism.org.au/monotropism-understanding-autistic-ways-of-being-through-the-lens-of-attention/
Date: 2025-09-30
Excerpt: "Monotropism is based on the idea that the mind functions as an interest system — we're all drawn to many things, and these interests help guide our attention. Autistic individuals often exhibit deep, sustained focus on a narrower range of interests or activities than non-autistic people."
Context: Monotropism theory offers a non-pathologizing alternative framework. Rather than viewing hyperfocus as a "symptom," it conceptualizes intense, singular focus as a different attentional style with both advantages (deep expertise, flow-like engagement) and disadvantages (difficulty with transitions, multi-tasking). Fergus Murray (2023) has extended this to ADHD, proposing "Kinetic Cognitive Style" (KCS) as a related attentional difference.[^31^]
Confidence: **Medium** (influential theory gaining traction but still emerging in peer-reviewed literature)

Claim: "Fergus Murray has further expanded on the concept of monotropism, proposing in 2023 that it may not be only characteristic in autism, but account for the overlap between ADHD — or Kinetic Cognitive Style (KCS) as they prefer — and autism."[^30^]
Source: Reframing Autism / Fergus Murray
URL: https://reframingautism.org.au/monotropism-understanding-autistic-ways-of-being-through-the-lens-of-attention/
Date: 2025-09-30
Excerpt: "Murray posits that if traits like impulsivity, inattention, and hyperactivity in ADHD exist on a continuum with, and in polarity to, Autistic inflexibility, hyperfocus, and inertia, it is possible that these continua are shaped by the same underlying monotropic attentional style."
Context: This is a speculative but theoretically significant proposal. If monotropism underlies both ADHD and autism, hyperfocus is not an ADHD-specific "symptom" but rather one pole of a universal attentional spectrum. This has major implications for how hyperfocus is framed clinically.
Confidence: **Low** (speculative; calls for further research explicitly acknowledged by author)

## 7. Key Research Gaps and Future Directions

Based on the reviewed literature, the following critical gaps were identified:

1. **Neuroimaging of Hyperfocus States:** No published fMRI or PET study has directly scanned individuals *during* hyperfocus episodes. All neurobiological inferences are indirect (resting-state connectivity, trait correlations, or structural differences).

2. **Causal Mechanisms:** It remains unknown whether hyperfocus is caused by excessive reward circuit activation, insufficient prefrontal inhibition, DMN suppression, or some combination. Causal studies (e.g., pharmacological manipulation, transcranial stimulation) are needed.

3. **Medication Effects:** Stimulant medications regulate dopamine and norepinephrine but their specific effects on hyperfocus frequency, intensity, and controllability are poorly characterized. One source claims medication doesn't eliminate hyperfocus but may reduce it by decreasing distractibility.[^32^]

4. **Developmental Trajectories:** The NIH 2024 study examined youth (ages 6-18). Whether hyperfocus-related connectivity patterns persist, normalize, or change in adulthood is unknown.

5. **Intervention Development:** No randomized controlled trial has tested interventions specifically targeting hyperfocus regulation in ADHD. Executive function training, neurofeedback, and behavioral strategies show promise but lack rigorous evidence.

6. **Cross-Cultural Validation:** The AHQ-D has been validated in U.S. adults. A Japanese version is under development. Cross-cultural replication is needed.
