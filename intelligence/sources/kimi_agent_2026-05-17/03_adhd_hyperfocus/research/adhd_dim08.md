## 1. Dopamine System

### 1.1 Volkow's Landmark PET Studies (JAMA 2009; Molecular Psychiatry 2011)

The most influential direct evidence for dopamine dysfunction in ADHD comes from Nora Volkow's group at the NIH, using positron emission tomography (PET) to quantify dopamine markers in vivo.

#### JAMA 2009: Evaluating Dopamine Reward Pathway in ADHD

Claim: "ADHD patients had lower levels of dopamine receptors and transporters in the accumbens and midbrain — two key regions of the brain directly involved in processing motivation and reward"[^1^]

Source: JAMA
URL: https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/185228
Date: September 9, 2009
Excerpt: "The results clearly showed that, relative to the healthy control subjects, the ADHD patients had lower levels of dopamine receptors and transporters in the accumbens and midbrain — two key regions of the brain directly involved in processing motivation and reward. In addition, the measurements of dopamine markers correlated with measures of behavior and clinical observations of ADHD symptoms, such as reduced levels of attention as measured by standard psychological tests."
Context: This study used PET imaging with [11C]raclopride (D2/D3 receptor marker) and [11C]cocaine (dopamine transporter marker) in 53 never-medicated adults with ADHD and 44 healthy controls.
Confidence: **High**

Claim: "Individuals with ADHD have a significant decrease in both of these markers in the dopamine areas associated with reward, motivation, and drive"[^2^]

Source: Medscape (reporting on Volkow JAMA 2009)
URL: https://www.medscape.com/viewarticle/708765
Date: September 11, 2009
Excerpt: "'Basically, this study showed that individuals with ADHD have a significant decrease in both of these markers in the dopamine areas associated with reward, motivation, and drive,' Dr. Volkow told Medscape Psychiatry. 'We also showed that the decreases in these markers were associated with the severity of inattention. In other words, the lower the concentration of these markers in these areas of the dopamine reward pathway, the more intense the symptoms of inattention.'"
Context: Direct quote from the primary investigator of the landmark study.
Confidence: **High**

**Key quantitative findings from Volkow et al. (2009):**
- Lower D2/D3 receptor availability in accumbens and midbrain in ADHD vs. controls
- Lower DAT availability in accumbens and midbrain in ADHD vs. controls
- Dopamine markers inversely correlated with inattention severity
- Disruption of synaptic dopamine markers also observed in caudate region

#### Molecular Psychiatry 2011: Motivation Deficit and Dopamine Reward Pathway Dysfunction

Claim: "Disruption of the dopamine reward pathway is associated with motivation deficits in ADHD adults, which may contribute to attention deficits"[^3^]

Source: Molecular Psychiatry (Nature Publishing Group)
URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20856250/
Date: November 2011
Excerpt: "Using positron emission tomography (PET), we showed decreased function in the brain dopamine reward pathway in adults with ADHD, which, we hypothesized, could underlie the motivation deficits in this disorder. To evaluate this hypothesis, we performed secondary analyses to assess the correlation between the PET measures of dopamine D2/D3 receptor and dopamine transporter availability (obtained with [(11)C]raclopride and [(11)C]cocaine, respectively) in the dopamine reward pathway (midbrain and nucleus accumbens) and a surrogate measure of trait motivation (assessed using the Achievement scale on the Multidimensional Personality Questionnaire or MPQ) in 45 ADHD participants and 41 controls."
Context: Secondary analysis of the 2009 JAMA cohort specifically testing the motivation hypothesis.
Confidence: **High**

**Key quantitative findings from Volkow et al. (2011):**
- Achievement scale lower in ADHD (11±5 vs. 14±3, P<0.001)
- D2/D3 receptors in accumbens correlated with Achievement (r=0.39, P<0.008)
- D2/D3 receptors in midbrain correlated with Achievement (r=0.41, P<0.005)
- DAT in accumbens correlated with Achievement (r=0.35, P<0.02)
- Achievement scores negatively correlated with inattention symptoms

### 1.2 Dopamine Receptor and Transporter Abnormalities

Claim: "Lower than normal DAT levels in left caudate and NAc in ADHD subjects and no difference in putamen"[^4^]

Source: NeuroImage (Elsevier)
URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S105381190601024X
Date: 2006 (published)
Excerpt: "These results do not corroborate higher DAT in ADHD but rather document lower than normal DAT levels in left caudate and NAc in ADHD subjects and no difference in putamen. This study also documents an association between DAT in putamen and inattention for both the ADHD and control groups. However, since equivalent levels of DAT in putamen are associated with 5 times higher ratings of inattention in the ADHD group relative to the control group, additional variable(s) in conjunction with DAT need to be considered."
Context: This study used a different radiotracer (PE2I) and found lower rather than higher DAT, contradicting some earlier hypotheses.
Confidence: **Medium** — conflicting with some other studies

### 1.3 DAT1 Gene (SLC6A3)

Claim: "Specific DAT1 variations are linked to symptoms of ADHD such as inattention, impulsivity, and medical treatments"[^5^]

Source: Journal of Scientific Research Writing (Rising Researchers)
URL: https://risingresearchers.com/journal/the-role-of-the-dopamine-transporter-gene-in-dopamine-dysregulation-in-attention-deficit-hyperactivity-disorder/
Date: Spring 2025
Excerpt: "The DAT1 gene codes for the dopamine transporter protein, which is responsible for the reuptake of dopamine from the synaptic cleft back into the presynaptic neuron. This process is crucial because of how important it is to maintain the balance of dopamine levels in the brain, especially in areas that are mainly associated with cognitive function, such as the prefrontal cortex. Variations or polymorphisms in the DAT1 gene, especially the 40-base pair variable number tandem repeat (VNTR) polymorphism, have been shown to impact the effectiveness of the reuptake of dopamine."
Context: Review article synthesizing evidence on DAT1's role in ADHD.
Confidence: **Medium** — review article, not primary research

Claim: "Individuals with certain DAT1 genotypes, specifically those with the 10R allele, exhibited higher trait scores of inattention"[^6^]

Source: Cited in Journal of Scientific Research Writing (referencing Bellgrove et al.)
URL: https://risingresearchers.com/journal/the-role-of-the-dopamine-transporter-gene-in-dopamine-dysregulation-in-attention-deficit-hyperactivity-disorder/
Date: Spring 2025
Excerpt: "Bellgrove et al. looked into additional information that could support the hypothesis that the DAT1 gene influences specific cognitive functions, particularly inattention and impulsivity. It was found that individuals with certain DAT1 genotypes, specifically those with the 10R allele, exhibited higher trait scores of inattention. These results support the idea that the DAT1 gene potentially influences cognitive processes such as attention, specifically in terms of spatial working memory (SWM)."
Context: Referencing Bellgrove et al.'s work on DAT1 10R allele and cognitive performance.
Confidence: **Medium**

### 1.4 DRD4 Gene

Claim: "The most consistent associations established between the gene encoding D4.7R and attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) and substance use disorders"[^7^]

Source: Frontiers in Endocrinology
URL: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2022.1014678/full
Date: September 14, 2022
Excerpt: "DRD4 polymorphisms have been associated with individual differences in impulse control-related neuropsychiatric disorders, with the most consistent associations found between the gene encoding D4.7R and attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) and substance use disorders (SUDs)."
Context: Major review on D4 receptor pharmacology and polymorphisms.
Confidence: **High**

Claim: "The D4.7R variant was less able to heteromerize with α2AR than D4.4R... heteromerization with D4.7R, but not with D4.4R, increases the potency of norepinephrine at activating α2AR"[^8^]

Source: Frontiers in Endocrinology
URL: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2022.1014678/full
Date: September 14, 2022
Excerpt: "The results of BRET experiments indicated that, akin to D2R-D4R heteromers, the D4.7R variant was less able to heteromerize with α2AR than D4.4R. Furthermore, results from radioligand-binding, CODA-RET and signaling experiments indicated that heteromerization with D4.7R, but not with D4.4R, increases the potency of norepinephrine at activating α2AR. Furthermore, D4.4R, but not D4.7R activation, allosterically inhibited α2AR-mediated signaling in their respective heteromers."
Context: Mechanistic explanation of how DRD4 7-repeat allele functionally differs from 4-repeat.
Confidence: **High**

### 1.5 Dopamine Transfer Deficit Theory (Tripp & Wickens)

Claim: "The dopamine transfer deficit (DTD) theory explains ADHD by means of an attenuated phasic dopamine response to cues (predictors) of expected rewards"[^9^]

Source: ADxS.org (compiling primary research)
URL: https://www.adxs.org/en/page/115/10-adhd-disorders-of-the-dopamine-system
Date: 2026
Excerpt: "The dopamine transfer deficit (DTD) theory explains ADHD by means of an attenuated phasic dopamine response to cues (predictors) of expected rewards, resulting in altered reinforcement sensitivity. If healthy people repeatedly receive a reward at the same time as a predictor, the dopamine neurons begin to fire at the predictor and the reward. If the relationship between reward and predictor is clear (learned), dopamine only fires to the predictor. In ADHD, the firing of dopamine cells is abnormal. Even when reward and predictor appeared together long enough to have been learned, people with ADHD still show too low a firing rate after the cue and an increased firing rate after the actual reward."
Context: Synthesis of Tripp & Wickens (2008) primary research.
Confidence: **Medium-High**

Claim: "In children with ADHD, the transfer of the dopamine response to previously neutral cues is disrupted... the anticipatory dopamine signal is weak or non-existent"[^10^]

Source: Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry (Wiley)
URL: https://acamh.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1469-7610.2007.01851.x
Date: 2008
Excerpt: "In children with ADHD, the transfer of the dopamine response to previously neutral cues is disrupted. In particular, the transfer of the dopamine cell response to the cue that predicts reinforcement fails to develop normally, so that the anticipatory dopamine signal is weak or non-existent."
Context: Primary source — Tripp & Wickens' original DTD theory paper.
Confidence: **High**

### 1.6 Reward Deficiency Syndrome (Blum)

Claim: "Genetic variants of dopaminergic genes and other 'reward genes' are important common determinants of reward deficiency syndrome (RDS), which we hypothesize includes ADHD as a behavioral subtype"[^11^]

Source: Neuropsychiatric Disease Treatment (Dove Press)
URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19183781/
Date: October 2008
Excerpt: "It has been proposed that genetic variants of dopaminergic genes and other 'reward genes' are important common determinants of reward deficiency syndrome (RDS), which we hypothesize includes ADHD as a behavioral subtype. We further hypothesize that early diagnosis through genetic polymorphic identification in combination with DNA-based customized nutraceutical administration to young children may attenuate behavioral symptoms associated with ADHD."
Context: Blum's theoretical framework linking ADHD to reward deficiency syndrome.
Confidence: **Low-Medium** — controversial, nutraceutical claims not widely accepted

### 1.7 Counter-Evidence: No Consistent Hypo-Dopaminergic State

Claim: "Recent PET/SPECT studies do not show predominant evidence supporting a hypo-dopaminergic state in ADHD, but rather conflicting results that imply both increased and decreased extracellular DA levels within frontostriatal regions"[^12^]

Source: Frontiers in Psychiatry
URL: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2024.1492126/full
Date: October 28, 2024
Excerpt: "There is no consistent neurophysiological support for a lack of frontostriatal DA in ADHD. Evidence exists for a decrease, increase, and no change in extracellular DA in ADHD relative to healthy controls... It may be possible to reconcile the hyper- vs. hypo-dopaminergic findings by considering dynamic versus generalized DA activity. For example, in a task-based study, Badgaiyan and colleagues reported decreased tonic but enhanced phasic release of DA in the right caudate of people with ADHD."
Context: Major 2024 review critically evaluating the dopamine hypothesis for ADHD.
Confidence: **High** — important counter-evidence

## 2. Norepinephrine System

### 2.1 Atomoxetine Mechanism: NET Inhibition and Prefrontal Dopamine Elevation

Claim: "Atomoxetine increases extracellular levels of norepinephrine and dopamine in prefrontal cortex of rat: a potential mechanism for efficacy in attention deficit/hyperactivity disorder"[^13^]

Source: Neuropsychopharmacology (Nature)
URL: https://www.nature.com/articles/1395936
Date: April 12, 2002
Excerpt: "We investigated the mechanism of action of atomoxetine in ADHD by evaluating the interaction of atomoxetine with monoamine transporters, the effect of acute atomoxetine on extracellular levels of NE and DA in the prefrontal cortex (PFC) and other noradrenergic and dopaminergic brain regions, and the effect of repeated administration of atomoxetine on the firing rate of noradrenergic cell bodies."
Context: Bymaster et al. (Lilly) — foundational preclinical paper on atomoxetine mechanism.
Confidence: **High**

Claim: "In the prefrontal cortex specifically, the norepinephrine transporter also handles dopamine reuptake. So by blocking NET, Atomoxetine effectively increases both norepinephrine and dopamine levels in this critical brain region"[^14^]

Source: MedFinder
URL: https://www.medfinder.com/blog/how-does-atomoxetine-work-mechanism-of-action-explained
Date: February 17, 2026
Excerpt: "In the prefrontal cortex specifically, the norepinephrine transporter also handles dopamine reuptake. So by blocking NET, Atomoxetine effectively increases both norepinephrine and dopamine levels in this critical brain region. This is important because dopamine is the neurotransmitter most associated with motivation, reward, and focus. Atomoxetine boosts dopamine where it matters most for ADHD — without flooding the entire brain's reward system the way stimulants can."
Context: This explains the "dopamine bonus" in PFC from NET blockade — the PFC lacks DAT, so NET handles DA reuptake.
Confidence: **High** (well-established mechanism)

### 2.2 Alpha-2A Adrenergic Receptors and Prefrontal Cortex Function

Claim: "Guanfacine acts within the PFC via post-synaptic α2A-AR on dendritic spines to inhibit cAMP-PKA-K+ channel signaling, thus strengthening network connectivity, enhancing PFC neuronal firing, and improving PFC cognitive functions"[^15^]

Source: Neurobiology of Learning and Memory (Elsevier)
URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1074742720301714
Date: October 21, 2021
Excerpt: "A variety of evidence shows that guanfacine acts within the PFC via post-synaptic α2A-AR on dendritic spines to inhibit cAMP-PKA-K+ channel signaling, thus strengthening network connectivity, enhancing PFC neuronal firing, and improving PFC cognitive functions. Although guanfacine's beneficial effects are present in rodent, they are especially evident in primates, where the PFC greatly expands and differentiates."
Context: Comprehensive review on guanfacine translational research by Arnsten group.
Confidence: **High**

Claim: "Functional loss of the α2A-AR subtype, unlike knock-out of the α2C-AR subtype, weakened performance of the prefrontal cortical task without affecting learning and resulted in loss of the beneficial response to guanfacine"[^16^]

Source: Journal of Neuroscience
URL: https://www.jneurosci.org/content/22/19/8771
Date: October 1, 2002
Excerpt: "We observed that functional loss of the α2A-AR subtype, unlike knock-out of the α2C-AR subtype, weakened performance of the prefrontal cortical task without affecting learning and resulted in loss of the beneficial response to guanfacine. These data demonstrate the importance of α2A-AR subtype stimulation for the cognitive functions of the prefrontal cortex and identify the molecular substrate for guanfacine and novel therapeutic interventions."
Context: Frang et al. (Arnsten lab) — genetic knockout study establishing α2A-AR as target.
Confidence: **High**

Claim: "The α2-AR agonist guanfacine increases regional cerebral blood flow in the dorsolateral PFC, the same brain region that is critical for performance of spatial working memory tasks"[^17^]

Source: Neuropsychopharmacology
URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0893133X00001111
Date: September 1, 2000
Excerpt: "Guanfacine treatment significantly improved cognitive performance of the working memory task, and significantly increased rCBF values in the dorsolateral PFC, the brain region most tightly associated with performance of spatial working memory tasks. In contrast, guanfacine had no significant effect on rCBF in the superior temporal cortex, an auditory association area unrelated to task performance."
Context: Avery et al. — SPECT imaging in monkeys demonstrating PFC-specific effects.
Confidence: **High**

### 2.3 Complementary Roles of Dopamine and Norepinephrine in PFC

Claim: "DA and NE have complementary beneficial actions with NA increasing the signal and DA reducing noise, and an optimal balance of both neurotransmitters is required for proper PFC functioning"[^18^]

Source: Psych Scene Hub
URL: https://psychscenehub.com/psychinsights/neurobiology-of-adhd/
Date: March 21, 2019
Excerpt: "At moderate levels, noradrenaline can improve prefrontal cortex functioning by stimulating postsynaptic α2A receptors. NA activation of the α2A receptor strengthens the neuronal signal and hence strengthens network connectivity... Stimulation of D1 receptors weakens the neuronal signal and is responsible for decreasing 'noise' by pruning inappropriate connections... Thus, DA and NE have complementary beneficial actions with NA increasing the signal and DA reducing noise, and an optimal balance of both neurotransmitters is required for proper PFC functioning."
Context: Summary of Arnsten's research on catecholamine modulation of PFC.
Confidence: **High**

### 2.4 Norepinephrine's Role in Arousal and the Cognitive-Energetic Model

Claim: "State-regulation hypotheses have gained much traction... a decreased ability to regulate arousal may contribute to the higher-level cognitive deficits in ADHD"[^19^]

Source: Frontiers in Psychiatry
URL: https://repositorio.uc.cl/server/api/core/bitstreams/98aefafa-3594-4502-80be-03074c85fa87/content
Date: Unknown
Excerpt: "State-regulation theory suggests that a decreased ability to regulate arousal may contribute to the higher-level cognitive deficits in ADHD. This theory constructed in the cognitive-energetic model emphasizes a deficit of energetic (alerting) factors among ADHD patients, leading to both executive dysfunction and hyperactivity symptoms."
Context: Review connecting locus coeruleus-norepinephrine arousal regulation to ADHD.
Confidence: **Medium-High**

## 3. Prefrontal Cortex

### 3.1 Shaw et al.: Delayed Cortical Maturation in ADHD (PNAS 2007)

Claim: "There was a marked delay in ADHD in attaining peak thickness throughout most of the cerebrum: the median age by which 50% of the cortical points attained peak thickness for this group was 10.5 years, which was significantly later than the median age of 7.5 years for typically developing controls"[^20^]

Source: Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences
URL: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0707741104
Date: December 4, 2007
Excerpt: "We found maturation to progress in a similar manner regionally in both children with and without ADHD, with primary sensory areas attaining peak cortical thickness before polymodal, high-order association areas. However, there was a marked delay in ADHD in attaining peak thickness throughout most of the cerebrum: the median age by which 50% of the cortical points attained peak thickness for this group was 10.5 years (SE 0.01), which was significantly later than the median age of 7.5 years (SE 0.02) for typically developing controls (log rank test chi(1)(2) = 5,609, P < 1.0 x 10(-20)). The delay was most prominent in prefrontal regions important for control of cognitive processes including attention and motor planning."
Context: Landmark study — 824 MRI scans from 223 children with ADHD and 223 controls. First neuroanatomic documentation of delayed cortical maturation in ADHD.
Confidence: **High**

Claim: "ADHD is characterized by delay rather than deviance in cortical maturation"[^21^]

Source: PNAS (Shaw et al. 2007 Discussion)
URL: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0707741104
Date: December 4, 2007
Excerpt: "Cortical development in children with ADHD lagged behind that of typically developing children by several years. However, the ordered sequence of regional development, with primary sensory and motor areas attaining their peak cortical thickness before high-order association areas, was similar in both groups, suggesting that ADHD is characterized by delay rather than deviance in cortical maturation. This contrasts with other neurodevelopmental disorders such as autism in which there appears to be a dramatic shift of brain growth curves."
Context: Critical distinction — ADHD shows delayed but normal sequence, unlike autism which shows deviant patterns.
Confidence: **High**

**Key quantitative details:**
- Overall cortical maturation delay: ~3 years (median peak age 10.5 vs. 7.5 years)
- Prefrontal cortex delay: median 10.4 years (ADHD) vs. 7.5 years (controls)
- Middle prefrontal cortex: ADHD peaked ~5 years after controls
- Superior/medial prefrontal: ADHD peaked ~2 years after controls
- Primary motor cortex: slightly EARLIER maturation in ADHD (7.0 vs. 7.4 years)

### 3.2 Cortical Thickness and Clinical Outcome (JAMA Psychiatry 2006)

Claim: "Children with a worse outcome have 'fixed' thinning of the left medial prefrontal cortex, which may compromise the anterior attentional network and encumber clinical improvement"[^22^]

Source: JAMA Psychiatry
URL: https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamapsychiatry/fullarticle/209556
Date: May 1, 2006
Excerpt: "Children with ADHD had global thinning of the cortex (mean reduction, −0.09 mm; P =.02), most prominently in the medial and superior prefrontal and precentral regions. Children with worse clinical outcome had a thinner left medial prefrontal cortex at baseline than the better outcome group (−0.38 mm; P =.003) and controls (−0.25 mm; P =.002). Cortical thickness developmental trajectories did not differ significantly between the ADHD and control groups throughout except in the right parietal cortex, where trajectories converged. This normalization of cortical thickness occurred only in the better outcome group."
Context: Longitudinal study of 163 children with ADHD followed for mean 5.7 years. Demonstrated outcome-dependent differences in cortical development.
Confidence: **High**

### 3.3 Developmental Trajectories into Adulthood

Claim: "Adult ADHD severity is associated with developmental trajectories of cerebral cortical regions... when the number of adult ADHD symptoms increased, particularly inattentive symptoms, the rate of cortical thinning in the medial and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex also increased"[^23^]

Source: SCDC Centre
URL: https://scdcentre.com/from-childhood-to-adulthood-trajectory-of-adhd-brain/
Date: May 15, 2019
Excerpt: "When the number of adult ADHD symptoms increased, particularly inattentive symptoms, the rate of cortical thinning in the medial and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex also increased... Remitted individuals were those whose symptoms decreased by adulthood to a level below the threshold for ADHD diagnosis... remitted ADHD adults tended to converge toward typical dimensions, in terms of structural and functional normalization of the cortex. However, persistent ADHD adults tended to show more fixed and non-progressive deficit."
Context: Reporting on Shaw et al. (2013) follow-up studies tracking ADHD into adulthood.
Confidence: **High**

### 3.4 Catecholamine Modulation and the Inverted-U in PFC

Claim: "Catecholamines exert inverted-U shaped modulatory actions within the PFC... optimal function is associated with moderate levels of catecholamine signaling while both inadequate and excessive signaling are associated with functional impairment"[^24^]

Source: Current Opinion in Behavioral Sciences (Elsevier)
URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2352154615000121
Date: August 2015
Excerpt: "Catecholamines exert inverted-U shaped modulatory actions within the PFC. Thus, optimal function is associated with moderate levels of catecholamine signaling while both inadequate and excessive signaling are associated with functional impairment. Research in primates engaged in working memory tasks has provided a useful model for examining the intracellular and circuit-level mechanisms associated with catecholamine modulation of PFC cognitive circuits."
Context: Arnsten & Berridge review on catecholamine mechanisms in PFC.
Confidence: **High**

Claim: "Optimal prefrontal dopamine signaling is linked with higher cognitive function" with "inverted quadratic fit... much stronger for prefrontal D1DRs alone, explaining 26% of the variance"[^25^]

Source: Behavioral Neuroscience (APA)
URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35389678/
Date: June 2022
Excerpt: "Our quantification of effect sizes for dopamine, D1DRs, and behavior revealed a negative quadratic slope. This is consistent with the proposed inverted U-shape of prefrontal dopamine and D1DRs and working memory performance, explaining 10% of the variance. Of note, the inverted quadratic fit was much stronger for prefrontal D1DRs alone, explaining 26% of the variance, compared to prefrontal dopamine alone, explaining 10% of the variance."
Context: Meta-analysis of 75 studies quantifying the inverted-U relationship between prefrontal dopamine/D1 receptors and working memory.
Confidence: **High**

### 3.5 Structural Brain Differences Summary

The most consistent structural findings in ADHD include[^26^]:
- Overall reduction in total brain size
- Smaller caudate nucleus and globus pallidus (high dopamine receptor density)
- Reduced ventral striatum (correlated with childhood hyperactivity/impulsivity)
- Thinner cortex in prefrontal regions, especially with DRD4 7-repeat allele
- Altered frontostriatal white matter (DTI)
- Cortical thinning associated with DRD4 7-repeat allele resolves in adolescence in better-outcome cases

Source: Psych Scene Hub / compilation of multiple studies
URL: https://psychscenehub.com/psychinsights/neurobiology-of-adhd/
Date: March 21, 2019
Confidence: **High**

## 4. Default Mode Network (DMN)

### 4.1 Castellanos & Proal: Meta-Analysis of 55 fMRI Studies (2012)

Claim: "The most consistent findings were that compared to controls, the ADHD groups exhibited hyperactivity in the DMN and hypoactivity in the task-positive networks such as the frontoparietal and ventral attentional networks during cognitive tasks"[^27^]

Source: American Journal of Psychiatry
URL: https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/10.1176/appi.ajp.2012.11101521
Date: 2012 (published); 2013 (retrieved)
Excerpt: "In children, ADHD-related hypoactivation was predominantly located in the ventral attention (44%) and frontoparietal (39%) networks, whereas hyperactivation was predominant in the default (37%), ventral attention (23%), and somatomotor (22%) networks... According to the default-mode hypothesis of ADHD, the default network might be inadequately regulated by other task-active systems and might consequently intrude on or disrupt ongoing cognitive performance, contributing to fluctuations in attention that characterize ADHD."
Context: Cortese et al. meta-analysis — 55 fMRI studies in children and adults with ADHD. Most influential synthesis of task-based fMRI findings.
Confidence: **High**

### 4.2 DMN Dysregulation in ADHD

Claim: "ADHD is linked to defects at these three levels (cognitive processes, energetic pools, and executive control)" with specific DMN-cognitive control network abnormalities[^28^]

Source: Frontiers in Human Neuroscience
URL: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/human-neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnhum.2022.859538/full
Date: May 20, 2022
Excerpt: "Disruptions of intrinsic brain network connectivity and task-based functional connectivity involving the default mode network (DMN), cingulo-opercular, frontoparietal and executive control are found to characterize ADHD. Structures of the cingulo-frontoparietal (CFP) attention network include frontostriatal and frontoparietal pathways, modulating attention, executive function including working memory, response inhibition, motor control and reward/motivation."
Context: Review of DMN findings in ADHD subtypes.
Confidence: **High**

### 4.3 DMN "Failure to Deactivate" and Mind-Wandering

Claim: "The inattentiveness observed in ADHD could be due to inadequate suppression of the DMN and its increased activity is associated with the intrusion of thoughts unrelated to the task or 'day dreaming'"[^29^]

Source: Brain and Behavior (Wiley)
URL: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/brb3.582
Date: September 28, 2016
Excerpt: "Sonuga-Barke and Castellanos (2007) suggest that the inattentiveness observed in ADHD could be due to inadequate suppression of the DMN and its increased activity is associated with the intrusion of thoughts unrelated to the task or 'day dreaming'. A meta-analysis of 55 ADHD fMRI task-based studies indicated that the most consistent findings were that compared to controls, the ADHD groups exhibited hyperactivity in the DMN and hypoactivity in the task-positive networks such as the frontoparietal and ventral attentional networks during cognitive tasks."
Context: Silberstein et al. — examining DMN modulation by methylphenidate.
Confidence: **High**

### 4.4 Castellanos & Aoki (2016): DMN Science in Development

Claim: "Current hypotheses implicate the interplay of default, cognitive control (frontoparietal) and attention (dorsal, ventral, salience) networks in ADHD; functional connectivities of reward-related and amygdala-related circuits are also supported as substrates for dimensional aspects of ADHD"[^30^]

Source: Biological Psychiatry: Cognitive Neuroscience and Neuroimaging
URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27713929/
Date: May 2016
Excerpt: "Current hypotheses implicate the interplay of default, cognitive control (frontoparietal) and attention (dorsal, ventral, salience) networks in ADHD; functional connectivities of reward-related and amygdala-related circuits are also supported as substrates for dimensional aspects of ADHD. Before these can be further specified and definitively tested, we assert the field must take on the challenge of mapping the 'topography' of the analytical space."
Context: Castellanos & Aoki narrative review — influential framing of ADHD as a disorder of network interactions rather than regional deficits.
Confidence: **High**

### 4.5 DMN and Methylphenidate

Claim: "MPH dramatically reduced the frontoparietal FC increase observed in the pre-MPH A-X blank interval rendering the ADHD FC changes similar to those seen in the control group"[^31^]

Source: Brain and Behavior (Wiley)
URL: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/brb3.582
Date: September 28, 2016
Excerpt: "MPH dramatically reduced the frontoparietal FC increase observed in the pre-MPH A-X blank interval rendering the ADHD FC changes similar to those seen in the control group. ## 5. Multi-Deficit Models

### 5.1 Dual Pathway Model (Sonuga-Barke 2002/2003)

Claim: "ADHD is the result of the dysregulation of action and thought resulting from poor inhibitory control associated with the meso-cortical branch of the dopamine system... In the other ADHD is a motivational style characterised by an altered delay of reward gradient linked to the meso-limbic dopamine branch"[^33^]

Source: Behavioural Brain Research (Elsevier)
URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11864715/
Date: 2002
Excerpt: "In one AD/HD is the result of the dysregulation of action and thought resulting from poor inhibitory control associated with the meso-cortical branch of the dopamine system projecting in the cortical control centres (e.g. pre-frontal cortex). In the other AD/HD is a motivational style characterised by an altered delay of reward gradient linked to the meso-limbic dopamine branch associated with the reward circuits (e.g. nucleus accumbens). The two pathways are further distinguished at the levels of symptoms, cognitive and motivation profiles and genetic and non-genetic origins."
Context: Sonuga-Barke's original dual pathway model — landmark theoretical paper.
Confidence: **High**

Claim: "Traditionally regarded as competing, EDF and DEL models have recently been presented as complimentary accounts of two psycho-patho-physiological subtypes of AD/HD with different developmental pathways, underpinned by different cortico-striatal circuits and modulated by different branches of the dopamine system"[^34^]

Source: Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews (Elsevier)
URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14624804/
Date: November 2003
Excerpt: "The currently dominant neuro-cognitive model of Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (AD/HD) presents the condition as executive dysfunction (EDF) underpinned by disturbances in the fronto-dorsal striatal circuit and associated dopaminergic branches (e.g. meso-cortical). In contrast, motivationally-based accounts focus on altered reward processes and implicate fronto-ventral striatal reward circuits and those meso-limbic branches that terminate in the ventral striatum especially the nucleus accumbens."
Context: Sonuga-Barke's elaborated dual pathway model with detailed neurocircuitry.
Confidence: **High**

**Neurocircuitry of the Dual Pathway Model:**
| Pathway | Circuit | Dopamine Branch | Key Region | Primary Deficit |
|---------|---------|----------------|------------|-----------------|
| Executive | Fronto-dorsal striatal | Meso-cortical | Prefrontal cortex | Inhibitory control |
| Motivational | Fronto-ventral striatal | Meso-limbic | Nucleus accumbens | Delay aversion |

### 5.2 Triple Pathway Model (Sonuga-Barke et al. 2010)

Claim: "Temporal processing, inhibitory control and delay-related deficits represented independent neuropsychological components... substantial groups of patients showing only one problem"[^35^]

Source: Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry
URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20410727/
Date: April 2010
Excerpt: "Temporal processing, inhibitory control and delay-related deficits represented independent neuropsychological components. ADHD children differed from controls on all factors. For ADHD patients, the co-occurrence of inhibitory, temporal processing and delay-related deficits was no greater than expected by chance with substantial groups of patients showing only one problem. Domain-specific patterns of familial co-segregation provided evidence for the validity of neuropsychological subgroupings."
Context: Sonuga-Barke et al. — first empirical support for adding temporal processing as a third pathway.
Confidence: **High**

**The Three Pathways:**
1. **Executive/Inhibitory Control** → Prefrontal cortex → Response inhibition, working memory
2. **Motivational/Delay Aversion** → Ventral striatum → Reward processing, temporal discounting
3. **Temporal Processing** → Cerebellum-frontal circuits → Timing, time estimation

### 5.3 Nigg's Multiple Deficit/Pathway Models

Claim: "Only a subgroup may have executive deficits... multiple pathways to ADHD"[^36^]

Source: Biological Psychiatry (Elsevier)
URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15949992/
Date: 2005
Excerpt: "Using executive functioning as an illustration of an issue that exists across all such theories, this article describes substantial overlaps in the group performance data. Thus only a subgroup may have executive deficits. Noted are other supportive data suggesting multiple pathways to ADHD. The article explores implications and recommends that future theory and research give more consideration to the probability that only a subset of behaviorally defined children will have a deficit in a given neurocognitive mechanism believed to contribute to the disorder."
Context: Nigg et al. — calling for heterogeneity-aware approaches to ADHD neuropsychology.
Confidence: **High**

### 5.4 Cognitive-Energetic Model (Sergeant 2000)

Claim: "The overall efficiency of information processing is determined by the interplay of three levels: cognitive processes, energetic pools, and management/executive function"[^37^]

Source: Frontiers in Psychology
URL: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.01991/full
Date: July 17, 2020
Excerpt: "The CEM proposes that the overall efficiency of information processing is determined by the interplay among three mechanisms at different levels, with top-down and bottom-up streams among them. The lower level includes four general stages of computational mechanisms of attention: encoding, search, decision, and motor organization. The middle level comprises the energetic pools: arousal, effort, and activation."
Context: Review of Sergeant's cognitive-energetic model.
Confidence: **Medium-High**

### 5.5 Tripartite Model Summary

Claim: "The tripartite pathway model explains the neuropsychological heterogeneity of ADHD as a combination of one or more deficits in inhibitory control, motivational control, and temporal processing"[^38^]

Source: Psych Scene Hub
URL: https://psychscenehub.com/psychinsights/neurobiology-of-adhd/
Date: March 21, 2019
Excerpt: "This model is a refinement of the dual pathway theory and explains the neuropsychological heterogeneity of ADHD as a combination of one or more deficits in inhibitory control, motivational control, and temporal processing. Three pathways are involved indicating ADHD subtypes: 1. Dysfunction of the prefrontal cortex is likely to result in a reduced ability to exert control. 2. Dysfunction in dorsal striatum might lead to differences in the ability to predict what events are going to occur, whereas dysfunction in ventral striatum is more likely to lead to deficits in motivation and reward processing. 3. Dysfunction of the cerebellum is likely associated with problems in the ability to predict when events are going to occur."
Context: Synthesis of Sonuga-Barke's tripartite model.
Confidence: **High**

## 6. Synthesis and Integration

### 6.1 The Catecholamine Consensus

The neurobiological consensus on ADHD can be summarized as follows:

**Hypofunctionality of catecholaminergic pathways projecting to the prefrontal cortex.** Both dopamine (via D1 receptors) and norepinephrine (via α2A receptors) are necessary for optimal PFC function. Dopamine reduces "noise" by pruning inappropriate connections, while norepinephrine strengthens "signal" by enhancing network connectivity[^18^]. Either too little or too much of either neurotransmitter impairs function (the inverted-U)[^24^].

### 6.2 Structural + Functional Integration

ADHD involves both:
1. **Structural differences**: Delayed cortical maturation[^20^], thinner PFC[^22^], smaller subcortical structures
2. **Functional differences**: Hypoactivation of frontoparietal and attention networks[^27^], hyperactivation (failure to suppress) of DMN[^29^], altered reward processing[^1^]

### 6.3 Multiple Independent Pathways

No single neurobiological deficit explains all ADHD cases. Evidence supports:
- **~40%** of ADHD children show delay aversion[^39^]
- Only a **subset** show clear executive deficits[^36^]
- Deficits in timing, motivation, and inhibition are **partially independent**[^35^]
- Different patients may have different combinations of these deficits

### 6.4 Stimulant Mechanism: Network-Level Integration

Claim: "Stimulant-induced modulation of dopamine and norepinephrine neurotransmission optimizes engagement of task-related brain networks, increases perceived saliency, and reduces interference from the default mode network"[^40^]

Source: Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews
URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0149763424003105
Date: January 25, 2025
Excerpt: "There is evidence that stimulant-induced modulation of dopamine and norepinephrine neurotransmission optimizes engagement of task-related brain networks, increases perceived saliency, and reduces interference from the default mode network. An acute administration of stimulants may reduce brain alterations observed in untreated individuals in fronto-striato-parieto-cerebellar networks during tasks or at rest."
Context: 2025 comprehensive review on stimulant pharmacodynamics in ADHD.
Confidence: **High**

## 7. Counter-Arguments and Limitations

### 7.1 Dopamine Hypothesis Challenges

The dopamine hypothesis has faced significant challenges:
- PET/SPECT studies show **conflicting results**: decreased, increased, AND no change in DA markers[^12^]
- Core DA-related genes (DRD4, DAT1) are **not among major risk genes** in GWAS studies[^12^]
- However, enrichment of ADHD risk variants in **genes expressed in midbrain dopaminergic neurons** has been found
- Humans with documented DA deficiency (rare metabolic diseases) have **motor but not typical ADHD symptoms**[^12^]

### 7.2 Delay Aversion Not Universal

Claim: "Only 40 percent of ADHD cases could be described as displaying DAv"[^39^]

Source: Exeter University (Delay Aversion and ADHD study)
URL: https://ore.exeter.ac.uk/ndownloader/files/56707106
Date: Unknown
Excerpt: "By using the 10th centile cut-off on the controls performance, only 40 percent of ADHD cases could be described as displaying DAv. Furthermore, each task and the DAv index as a whole had only moderate operating characteristics identifying between 60 and 70% of cases correctly."
Context: Only a minority of ADHD children show clear delay aversion.
Confidence: **High**

### 7.3 Heterogeneity of Neuropsychological Deficits

Claim: "11% of patients did not show neuropsychological dysfunctioning" and "adult ADHD is neuropsychologically heterogeneous"[^41^]

Source: European Neuropsychopharmacology
URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0924977X15002631
Date: November 18, 2016
Excerpt: "Compared to healthy adults, patients with ADHD showed impaired EF, were more impulsive, and more variable in responding. However, effect sizes were small to moderate (range: 0.05–0.70) and 11% of patients did not show neuropsychological dysfunctioning. The best fitting model predicting ADHD included measures from distinct cognitive domains (82.1% specificity, 64.9% sensitivity)."
Context: Large adult ADHD neuropsychological study demonstrating substantial heterogeneity.
Confidence: **High**

### 7.4 Alternative Frameworks

Some researchers propose alternative interpretations of DMN findings:

Claim: "ADHD brains maintain human attention capacity that neurotypical brains more readily suppress. Rather than deficit preventing default mode network suppression, ADHD represents resistance to suppression demands"[^42^]

Source: Neurodiversity Directory
URL: https://neurodiversity.directory/adhd-mind-wandering-default-mode-network/
Date: February 26, 2026
Excerpt: "Alternative interpretation: ADHD brains maintain human attention capacity that neurotypical brains more readily suppress. Rather than deficit preventing default mode network suppression, ADHD represents resistance to suppression demands... The default mode network keeps activating. Internal mentation continues. Self-referential thought persists."
Context: Neurodiversity-informed reframing of DMN findings. Note: not peer-reviewed.
Confidence: **Low** — interesting perspective but not empirically validated
