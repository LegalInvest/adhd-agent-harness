## 1. The Pre-History (1798–1902)

### Finding 1.1: Alexander Crichton Preceded Still by a Century

Before George Still's 1902 lectures, Scottish physician Sir Alexander Crichton described attention disorders in his 1798 work *"An Inquiry Into the Nature and Origin of Mental Derangement"*.

```
Claim: Crichton described a condition involving "the incapacity of attending with a necessary degree of constancy to any one object" — predating Still by over a century.
Source: Lange et al., "The history of attention deficit hyperactivity disorder" / Verywell Mind ADHD History
URL: https://www.verywellmind.com/adhd-history-of-adhd-2633127
Date: 2010/2026
Excerpt: "1798: Sir Alexander Crichton, a Scottish physician, observes a disorder resembling ADHD. ADHD as we know it won't be defined as a diagnosis until 1987, nearly two centuries later."
Confidence: high
```

## 2. George Still and "Defect of Moral Control" (1902)

### Finding 2.1: Still's Goulstonian Lectures — The "Scientific Starting Point"

In March 1902, Sir George Frederic Still delivered his Goulstonian Lectures before the Royal College of Physicians of London, titled *"On Some Abnormal Psychical Conditions in Children."* These are universally recognized as the first scientific clinical description of what would become ADHD.

```
Claim: Still described 43 children with "an abnormal defect of moral control" — 15 boys and 5 girls with the condition "without general impairment of intellect and without physical disease." He observed symptoms before age 7 in most cases and noted a 3:1 male-to-female ratio.
Source: Lange, Reichl, Lange, Tucha & Tucha, "The history of attention deficit hyperactivity disorder" — PMC / Atten Defic Hyperact Disord
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3000907/
Date: 2010
Excerpt: "Still describes 'the particular psychical conditions (...) which are concerned with an abnormal defect of moral control in children' (Still 1902, p. 1008). He defines moral control as 'the control of action in conformity with the idea of the good of all'... Still divides these cases in two further groups, children with a 'morbid defect of moral control associated with physical disease'... and children with a 'defect of moral control as a morbid manifestation, without general impairment of intellect and without physical disease'"
Confidence: high
```

### Finding 2.2: Still's Specific Symptom Descriptions

Still's descriptions of inattention are strikingly modern:

```
Claim: Still described children with "a quite abnormal incapacity for sustained attention" who would put boots on wrong feet without noticing, or repeat "Good-night" several times unaware they had done so.
Source: Lange et al. / PMC
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3000907/
Date: 2010
Excerpt: "the case of a boy with moral defect who would repeat the process of saying 'Good-night' several times before he was aware that he had done so; the same boy would often put his boot on the wrong foot apparently without noticing it. Another boy, aged six years, with marked moral defect was unable to keep his attention even to a game for more than a very short time"
Confidence: high
```

### Finding 2.3: Still's "Keynote" — Immediate Gratification

```
Claim: Still identified "self-gratification, the immediate gratification of self without regard either to the good of others or to the larger and more remote good of self" as the keynote — a concept modern researchers link to delay of gratification deficits in ADHD.
Source: Lange et al. / PMC
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3000907/
Date: 2010
Excerpt: "The keynote of these qualities is self-gratification, the immediate gratification of self without regard either to the good of others or to the larger and more remote good of self. (Still, 1902, p. 1009)"
Confidence: high
```

**⚠️ KEY TURNING POINT #1 (1902):** George Still distinguishes children with behavioral control problems but normal intelligence from those with intellectual disability — creating the conceptual space for what would become ADHD.

## 3. The Encephalitis Lethargica Epidemic (1917–1928)

### Finding 3.1: Post-Encephalitic Behavior Disorder Shaped ADHD Concepts

The 1917–1928 encephalitis lethargica epidemic ("sleeping sickness") affected approximately 20 million people worldwide. Surviving children displayed post-encephalitic behavior disorders that closely resembled hyperactivity and inattention.

```
Claim: The encephalitis lethargica epidemic popularized the view that attentional and disruptive behavioral symptoms could be caused by brain damage — directly influencing the "minimal brain dysfunction" concept that would dominate ADHD thinking for decades.
Source: Lange et al. / Psychology Today
URL: https://www.psychologytoday.com/gb/blog/finding-purpose/202101/is-adhd-real-disorder-or-one-end-normal-continuum
Date: 2021
Excerpt: "Many of the affected children who survived this brain infection subsequently displayed significantly abnormal behavior, which was referred to as 'postencephalitic behavior disorder.' This quite often included hyperactivity and distractibility... Postencephalitic behavior disorder aroused interest in hyperactivity in children and influenced the scientific development of the concept that became ADHD"
Confidence: high
```

**⚠️ KEY TURNING POINT #2 (1917–1928):** The encephalitis epidemic created the "brain damage → hyperactivity" causal model that would dominate ADHD etiological thinking for 50+ years.

## 4. Charles Bradley and the Benzedrine Accident (1937)

### Finding 4.1: The Accidental Discovery

In 1937, psychiatrist Charles Bradley at the Emma Pendleton Bradley Home in Providence, Rhode Island, administered Benzedrine sulfate to children with behavioral disorders to treat headaches. He accidentally discovered dramatic behavioral improvements.

```
Claim: Bradley's 1937 study was the first documented use of a stimulant (Benzedrine/amphetamine) for children with behavioral problems. The drug improved school performance, social interactions, and emotional responses — but his work was "largely ignored for nearly 25 years."
Source: Strohl, "Bradley's Benzedrine Studies on Children with Behavioral Disorders" — Yale J Biol Med / PMC
URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3064242/
Date: 2011
Excerpt: "In 1937, psychiatrist Charles Bradley administered Benzedrine sulfate, an amphetamine, to 'problem' children... however, Bradley noticed an unexpected effect upon the behavior of the children: improved school performance, social interactions, and emotional responses... Bradley's studies went largely ignored in the field of child psychiatry for nearly 25 years."
Confidence: high
```

### Finding 4.2: The Paradoxical "Subdued" Response

```
Claim: Half of Bradley's subjects became "distinctly subdued" on a stimulant — a paradoxical response that Bradley could not explain. This remains relevant to understanding stimulant mechanisms in ADHD today.
Source: Strohl / PMC
URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3064242/
Date: 2011
Excerpt: "Half of the children in both studies exhibited a 'distinctly subdued' response. For example, irritable, aggressive, and noisy children became more placid, easy-going, and interested in their surroundings... Bradley could not justify the paradox of a stimulant drug producing a subdued response"
Confidence: high
```

### Finding 4.3: Bradley's Warning About Over-Reliance on Drugs

```
Claim: Bradley explicitly warned that drugs should only play a supporting role, stating they could not cure underlying causes. Modern psychiatry largely ignored this caution.
Source: Strohl / PMC
URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3064242/
Date: 2011
Excerpt: "Bradley was adamant that drugs should only play a supporting role in a holistic approach to treatment. Bradley did not foresee the extent to which modern psychiatry would focus only on the potential of Benzedrine's effects and ignore Bradley's own conclusions."
Confidence: high
```

**⚠️ KEY TURNING POINT #3 (1937):** Charles Bradley accidentally discovers that stimulants improve behavior in "problem children" — establishing the pharmacological template for ADHD treatment that would remain essentially unchanged for 85+ years.

## 5. From Brain Damage to Dysfunction (1940s–1960s)

### Finding 5.1: Minimal Brain Damage → Minimal Brain Dysfunction

The post-encephalitic model evolved into the "minimal brain damage" concept, later softened to "minimal brain dysfunction" (MBD) when no actual brain lesions could be found.

```
Claim: The MBD concept was formalized by Clements in 1966, describing "children of near average, average, or above average general intelligence with certain learning or behavioral disabilities... associated with deviations of function of the central nervous system."
Source: Mather & Wires, "Understanding, Educating, and Supporting Children with Specific Learning Disabilities" / PMC
URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6851403/

Confidence: high
```

```
Confidence: high
```

## 6. DSM-I: The Absence (1952)

### Finding 6.1: ADHD Did Not Exist in DSM-I

The first edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual (DSM-I, 1952) did not include any diagnosis resembling ADHD. It referenced "chronic brain syndromes of unknown cause" that included some behavioral presentations we now recognize as hyperactivity, but no specific category.

```
Claim: DSM-I (1952) did not include ADHD or any equivalent diagnosis. The condition first entered official psychiatric nosology only in DSM-II (1968).
Source: Verywell Mind / Adult ADHD: Underdiagnosis of a Treatable Condition / PMC
URL: https://www.verywellmind.com/adhd-history-of-adhd-2633127
Date: 2026
Excerpt: "1952: The first edition of the 'Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders' (DSM) is published by the American Psychiatric Association (APA) but does not mention an ADHD-like disorder."
Confidence: high
```

**⚠️ KEY TURNING POINT #4 (1952):** ADHD does NOT exist in official psychiatric nosology. The concept exists only in research literature.

## 7. DSM-II: Psychoanalytic Framing (1968)

### Finding 7.1: "Hyperkinetic Reaction of Childhood" — Still Psychoanalytic

DSM-II (1968) was the first official inclusion, but heavily influenced by psychoanalytic thinking. The disorder was conceptualized as a "reaction" to psychological trauma or parent-child relational issues.

```
Claim: DSM-II introduced "hyperkinetic reaction of childhood" — focusing primarily on excessive motor activity, with the APA "heavily influenced by psychoanalytic thinking, which tended to assume that mental disorders were reactions to childhood psychological trauma or parent-child relational issues."
Source: Psychology Today / Neuropsychiatry (London)
URL: https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/finding-purpose/202101/is-adhd-real-disorder-or-one-end-normal-continuum
Date: 2021
Excerpt: "In 1968, the second edition of the American Psychiatric Association's Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-II) renamed it 'hyperkinetic reaction of childhood' (The APA was at that time heavily influenced by psychoanalytic thinking, which tended to assume that mental disorders were reactions to childhood psychological trauma or parent-child relational issues)"
Confidence: high
```

**⚠️ KEY TURNING POINT #5 (1968):** ADHD enters official nosology for the first time — but as a psychoanalytic "reaction" rather than a neurobiological condition.

## 8. The Turning Point: DSM-III and Virginia Douglas (1980)

### Finding 8.1: Virginia Douglas Shifts Focus from Hyperactivity to Attention

Canadian psychologist Virginia Douglas published a landmark 1972 paper arguing that attention deficit, not hyperactivity, was the core feature of the disorder.

```
Claim: Douglas (1972) argued that "attention deficit" should be viewed as more significant than hyperactivity, positing that "a core group of symptoms involving inability to sustain attention and to control impulsivity can account for most of the deficits found in the hyperactive group."
Source: Epstein et al., "Fifty years of executive control research in ADHD" / ScienceDirect
URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S014976342300430X
Date: 2023
Excerpt: "Virginia Douglas (Douglas, 1972, pp. 259) posited that 'a core group of symptoms involving inability to sustain attention and to control impulsivity can account for most of the deficits found in the hyperactive group.'"
Confidence: high
```

### Finding 8.2: Robert Spitzer's DSM-III Revolution

DSM-III (1980), led by Robert Spitzer, marked a radical break from psychoanalytic thinking. It introduced operationalized criteria and renamed the disorder "Attention Deficit Disorder (ADD)" with two subtypes.

```
Claim: DSM-III (1980), under Robert Spitzer's leadership, was a "compromise solution" between psychoanalysts and biological psychiatrists that "sacrificed validity in some instances for agreement" and created a "cookbook" style manual that invited "diagnostic inflation."
Source: The perils of diagnostic inflation / Nature
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3537802/
Date: 2013
Excerpt: "Spitzer adopted a system that was designed to particularly remove inference, relying solely on what you see... DSM-III sacrificed validity in some instances for agreement. Reliability after all is an absolute prerequisite for validity."
Confidence: high
```

### Finding 8.3: The Two Subtypes of DSM-III

```
Claim: DSM-III introduced two subtypes: "Attention Deficit Disorder with Hyperactivity" (replacing DSM-II's hyperkinetic reaction) and "Attention Deficit Disorder without Hyperactivity" (a new subtype with no DSM-II equivalent). This was the first official recognition that attention problems could exist without hyperactivity.
Source: The scientific integrity of ADHD / PMC
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9811381/
Date: 2021
Excerpt: "Spitzer and Cantwell reported on the basic features of DSM-III classification. They explained that ADD was presented in DSM-III as having two subtypes: 'attention deficit disorder with hyperactivity and attention deficit disorder without hyperactivity'... ADD with hyperactivity replaced DSM-II's diagnosis of hyperkinetic reaction of childhood or adolescence, but there was no DSM-II equivalent for ADD without hyperactivity."
Confidence: high
```

**⚠️ KEY TURNING POINT #6 (1980):** DSM-III revolutionizes psychiatric diagnosis. Robert Spitzer replaces psychoanalytic "reactions" with operationalized, descriptive criteria. Virginia Douglas's attention-focused model becomes codified. The disorder is renamed ADD.

## 9. DSM-III-R: The Name Becomes ADHD (1987)

### Finding 9.1: Elimination of ADD Without Hyperactivity

DSM-III-R (1987) controversially renamed the disorder "Attention-Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder" and eliminated the ADD without hyperactivity subtype, replacing it with "Undifferentiated ADHD."

```
Claim: DSM-III-R (1987) renamed the condition ADHD, removed subtypes, and effectively eliminated the diagnosis for individuals without hyperactive behavior — a decision later reversed in DSM-IV.
Source: Neuropsychiatry (London) / Verywell Mind
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3955126/
Date: 2013
Excerpt: "The term Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) was introduced in DSM-III-R, with the controversial elimination of ADD without Hyperactivity."
Confidence: high
```

**⚠️ KEY TURNING POINT #7 (1987):** The name "ADHD" is officially born. The elimination of the non-hyperactive subtype effectively made invisible the presentation most common in girls — contributing to decades of gender-biased underdiagnosis.

## 10. DSM-IV: Three Subtypes (1994)

### Finding 10.1: Subtypes Return, Defined by Symptom Counts

DSM-IV (1994), chaired by Allen Frances, reintroduced three subtypes based on symptom counts in two dimensions: inattention (9 symptoms) and hyperactivity-impulsivity (9 symptoms).

```
Claim: DSM-IV introduced three subtypes: Predominantly Inattentive Type (ADHD-I), Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive Type (ADHD-H), and Combined Type (ADHD-C) — defined by thresholds of 6+ symptoms in each domain.
Source: Willcutt et al., "Validity of DSM-IV ADHD symptom dimensions and subtypes" / J Abnorm Psychol
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3622557/
Date: 2012
Excerpt: "Diagnostic criteria for ADHD in the fourth edition of the DSM (DSM-IV; American Psychiatric Association, 1994) defined three nominal subtypes based on differential elevations on two dimensions of nine symptoms of inattention and nine symptoms of hyperactivity-impulsivity."
Confidence: high
```

### Finding 10.2: Subtypes Lack Longitudinal Validity

```
Claim: A comprehensive meta-analysis of 546 studies concluded that DSM-IV subtypes "do not identify discrete subgroups with sufficient long-term stability to justify the classification of distinct forms of the disorder" — they are "a convenient clinical shorthand" rather than valid categories.
Source: Willcutt et al. / J Abnorm Psychol
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3622557/
Date: 2012
Excerpt: "the DSM-IV ADHD subtypes provide a convenient clinical shorthand to describe the functional and behavioral correlates of current levels of inattention and hyperactivity-impulsivity symptoms, but do not identify discrete subgroups with sufficient long-term stability to justify the classification of distinct forms of the disorder."
Confidence: high
```

**⚠️ KEY TURNING POINT #8 (1994):** DSM-IV formally recognizes inattentive ADHD as a subtype — making the "lost generation" of girls potentially diagnosible. But the subtype model lacks longitudinal validity.

## 11. DSM-5: Adult Recognition and Widened Gates (2013)

### Finding 11.1: Age of Onset Raised from 7 to 12

DSM-5 made two major changes to the age criterion: raising onset from "before age 7" to "before age 12," and removing the requirement for symptoms to cause impairment at onset (just requiring symptoms to be present).

```
Claim: DSM-5 changed the age of onset criterion from "some hyperactive-impulsive or inattentive symptoms that caused impairment were present before age 7 years" to "several inattentive or hyperactive-impulsive symptoms present prior to age 12 years" — both changes widen the diagnostic net.
Source: A review of changes to the attention deficit/hyperactivity disorder criteria in DSM-5
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6849294/
Date: 2018
Excerpt: "The modification thus comprised two changes: increasing the age of onset of symptoms from before 7 years to before 12 years, and removing the requirement for the 'onset of symptoms causing impairment' to the 'onset of symptoms'. Both changes widen the definition of ADHD and potentially lead to the widening of treatment recommendations."
Confidence: high
```

### Finding 11.2: Adult ADHD Formal Recognition

```
Claim: DSM-5 was the first to include explicit criteria for adult ADHD diagnosis, modify symptom descriptions for adult applicability, and allow comorbid diagnosis with Autism Spectrum Disorder (previously excluded).
Source: Cochrane Library / Multiple sources
URL: https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD013011.pub2/es
Date: 2021
Excerpt: "The diagnostic criteria for ADHD in the DSM-5 was amended to include the criteria for a diagnosis of ADHD in adults... the age of onset of the first symptoms was increased to 12 years, which reduced the diagnostic threshold for individuals aged 17 or older... Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) was no longer an exclusionary diagnosis"
Confidence: high
```

### Finding 11.3: Allen Frances's Critique of DSM-5 ADHD Changes

```
Claim: Allen Frances, chair of DSM-IV, became one of DSM-5's fiercest critics, calling diagnostic inflation "rampant" and noting the "tripling of ADD in the last 10 years." He specifically called out ADHD as one of "three new false epidemics of mental disorder in children."
Source: Frances, "Saving Normal" / Psychology Today review
URL: https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/reclaiming-childhood/201407/review-saving-normal-0
Date: 2014
Excerpt: "Frances is particularly concerned about the exponential increase in the diagnosis of psychiatric conditions in children, writing: 'We failed to predict or prevent three new false epidemics of mental disorder in children – autism, attention deficit, and childhood bipolar disorder. And we did nothing to contain the rampant diagnostic inflation that was already expanding the boundary of psychiatry far beyond its competence.'"
Confidence: high
```

**⚠️ KEY TURNING POINT #9 (2013):** DSM-5 widens the diagnostic net significantly — raising onset age to 12, removing impairment-at-onset requirement, recognizing adult ADHD, and allowing ASD comorbidity. Allen Frances calls this diagnostic inflation.

## 12. ICD-10 vs. ICD-11: The WHO Divergence

### Finding 12.1: ICD-10's "Hyperkinetic Disorder" Was Narrower Than DSM

```
Claim: ICD-10 (1992) used "Hyperkinetic Disorders" (F90) requiring impaired attention, hyperactivity, early onset before age 6, and long duration — with anxiety, mood disorders, and ASD excluded. Prevalence estimates using ICD-10 were 4.1% lower than DSM-IV.
Source: Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder: A Historical Neuropsychological Perspective / PMC
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5724393/
Date: 2024
Excerpt: "ICD-10 (European Description) F90: Hyperkinetic Disorders. Requires impaired attention, hyperactivity, early onset (before age 6 years), and long duration. Anxiety and mood disorders and ASD must be excluded."
Confidence: high
```

### Finding 12.2: ICD-11 Adopts DSM-5's Widened Framework

```
Claim: ICD-11 (approved 2019, effective 2022) replaced "hyperkinetic disorder" with "6A05 Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder," adopting DSM-5's broader approach including adult diagnosis and the age 12 criterion. ICD-11 criteria are "vaguer and less clearly defined" than DSM-5, potentially resulting in more diagnoses.
Source: The reclassification of neurodevelopmental disorders in ICD-11 / PMC
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12638323/
Date: 2025
Excerpt: "the ICD-10 terminology of hyperkinetic disorder... is replaced by ADHD... Compared to the DSM-5, the criteria for diagnosing ADHD are vaguer and less clearly defined, potentially resulting in more diagnoses. While the age criterion was 5 years in the ICD-10, the critical time window has now been raised to 12 years... This allows for a broader and later initial diagnosis, which will lead to an increase in prevalence."
Confidence: high
```

**⚠️ KEY TURNING POINT #10 (2019/2022):** WHO's ICD-11 abandons the narrower European "hyperkinetic disorder" concept and adopts the broader American ADHD framework — globalizing the widened diagnostic net.

### The Archaeological Layers of ADHD

Layer 1 (1902–1917): **Moral deviance** — Still's "defect of moral control"
Layer 2 (1917–1960s): **Brain damage** — post-encephalitic → minimal brain damage/dysfunction
Layer 3 (1960s–1980): **Psychoanalytic reaction** — DSM-II's "hyperkinetic reaction of childhood"
Layer 4 (1980–1987): **Attention deficit** — DSM-III's ADD with/without hyperactivity
Layer 5 (1987–1994): **Consolidated hyperactivity** — DSM-III-R's unitary ADHD
Layer 6 (1994–2013): **Heterogeneous subtypes** — DSM-IV's three subtypes
Layer 7 (2013–present): **Lifespan neurodevelopmental condition** — DSM-5/ICD-11's presentations, adult criteria, widened age windows

Each layer did not replace but **accumulated** upon the previous — creating the palimpsest we call "ADHD" today.
