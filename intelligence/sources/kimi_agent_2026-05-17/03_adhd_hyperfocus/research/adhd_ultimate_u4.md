## Methodology Notes

### CONFIRMED Criteria (Table 1)
- **Self-disclosed:** The individual has publicly stated they have ADHD in an interview, memoir, documentary, social media post, or official statement
- **Medically confirmed:** A diagnosis has been confirmed by medical professionals and disclosed by the individual or their authorized representatives
- **Court/documentary evidence:** Official court documents or BBC/major network documentaries confirm the diagnosis

### LIKELY Criteria (Table 2)
- **Second-party claim from credible source:** A reputable organization (e.g., ADHD Foundation, NHS wellbeing pack) has stated the individual has ADHD, but the individual has not personally spoken openly
- **Self-speculated retrospective:** The individual has said they "would probably have been diagnosed" or "would probably have been labelled" with ADHD as a child, but no formal diagnosis confirmed

### SPECULATED Criteria (Table 3)
- **Posthumous diagnosis:** Retrospective claims about deceased historical figures with no medical records
- **Third-party armchair diagnosis:** Journalists, bloggers, or pop-psychology sources attributing ADHD to public figures without the individual's acknowledgment
- **Trait-based speculation:** Claims based solely on behavioral observations or biographical anecdotes

## Confidence Legend

- **HIGH:** Primary source directly quotes the individual or their authorized representative; medical documentation or court records support the claim
- **MEDIUM:** Second-party claim from credible organization, or self-speculated retrospective without formal diagnosis
- **LOW:** Third-party speculation, posthumous armchair diagnosis, or conflation with other neurodevelopmental conditions
- **VERY LOW:** Student essays, non-authoritative blogs, zero primary sourcing, or pure trait-based speculation
